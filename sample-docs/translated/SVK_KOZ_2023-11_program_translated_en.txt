[PAGE 2 | PRIORITIES]. Trade union. INTRODUCTION. The trade union's interest lies in the consistent defense of the rights and interests of employees. The trade union rejects all attempts to restrict or eliminate acquired rights, reduce the level of protection for employees, or lower their standard of living. The trade union's ambition is to actively participate in ensuring that labor law protects employees by providing them with adequate protection by law and decent working conditions, especially fair wages and salaries, safe working conditions, equal treatment, professional development, and the ability to balance their work and family life. The trade union is the largest organized interest group in Slovakia and is part of the largest movement - the labor movement - in Europe and the world. The following priorities, which stem from the values and program pillars and the trade union's commitment to European and national trade union structures, as well as various position papers and opinions, include so-called "traditional" (essential) issues such as wage and salary increases, bonuses, minimum wage, labor law, the social security system, including the pension system, reducing working hours, balancing work and family life, a healthy work environment, improving the work environment, eliminating psychosocial risks in the workplace, social dialogue, and collective bargaining, trade union rights, strengthening employment, a labor-friendly tax policy, education policy, and its connection to the needs of the labor market, and more. Technological progress, digitalization, demographic changes, and the climate crisis will generate enormous changes not only in the world of work but also in the way individuals and societies live. As a result of these developments, we can expect (social) risks in the form of job losses, an increase in structural unemployment, a decreasing ability of new sectors and industries to create jobs, a decreasing ability of employees to compete with technology through education, increased technological unemployment, a deepening of income inequality, an increase in poverty and social exclusion associated with (new and "newer") social risks, increased unemployment, increased pressure on the social security system and its financing, the emergence of (new) forms of precarious work and an increase in precarious jobs, a reduction in the share of labor in national income, a reduction in wages, working conditions, and social standards, and a subsequent weakening of social security schemes, monopolization, an increase in the shadow economy and tax evasion, inadequate protection of employees in newly created jobs in new industries (trade union, labor law), and an increase in working poverty. These scenarios may materialize if public policies, including and especially social policies, do not respond promptly and adequately to the changes in development. These issues must also be considered in the broader context of education, healthcare, housing, modernization, support for innovation, science, and research, regional policy, and addressing the climate crisis, all of which are necessary for sustainable economic growth and a strong social state. The trade union is interested in the government guaranteeing the maintenance and strengthening of social rights and security, social inclusion, justice, and equality, decent wages and salaries, guaranteed social and health security, quality public services, a fair tax mix, and a fair transition to a knowledge-based, socially and environmentally sustainable economy. The trade union believes that political representation should focus its attention and implement policies aimed at a new economic growth model that supports industries with high added value, promotes employment and the creation of quality jobs, improves the working environment by minimizing precarious forms of work and protecting employees in the workplace, reduces working hours and balances work and private life, supports lifelong learning, gender equality, equal opportunities, diversity, and inclusion. The trade union rejects all forms of discrimination and violence in the workplace, the strengthening of right-wing extremism, as well as attacks on democracy, civil, and social rights. The trade union is interested in Slovakia being strongly integrated into the European Union, which, among other things...

2. WAGES, REMUNERATION, AND COLLECTIVE BARGAINING
The trade union believes that raising wages and moving towards economically and socially advanced European countries is essential for strengthening the Slovak economy. Wage increases and the associated increase in the purchasing power of the population are crucial for revitalizing the domestic market and supporting services. The Slovak economic model, based on low wages and attracting foreign direct investment, is exhausted. The implementation of the EU directive on adequate minimum wages will bring several challenges in the field of remuneration and collective bargaining in the near future. Remuneration in the public sector is not adequately addressed, and wage supplements and minimum wage requirements under the Labor Code are also problematic.
Priorities:
- Allowing social partners to maintain autonomy in the process of negotiating minimum wages, with the possibility of reaching an agreement.
- Establishing a so-called "automatic" minimum wage adjustment mechanism.
- Reassessing the so-called "automatic" mechanism for calculating the minimum wage, especially in the context of implementing the EU directive on adequate minimum wages. It is reasonable to assume that the current method of calculating the minimum wage ("automatic") does not ensure the achievement of the recommended proportions within the reference values in the European directive, which are the comparison of the minimum wage with the average wage and the median within one year.
The trade union has long called for a return to a minimum wage that is at least 60% of the average wage, or for setting the minimum wage in accordance with the recommended reference framework defined in the EU directive on adequate minimum wages.
- Setting wage supplements:
In the event of further interventions in wage supplements determined by the Labor Code, it must be ensured that they are reasonable and regularly indexed. We consider wage supplements to be a tool for compensating employees who must perform work during non-standard working hours, often at the expense of their personal lives and health. The unprecedentedly high number of hours worked in such working conditions must be adequately compensated. Furthermore, the cost implications may positively impact the transformation of the economy towards a model that is not primarily based on the volume of work performed during non-standard working hours.
- Minimum wage requirements:
Increasing the values based on coefficients and improving their enforceability.
The amendment to the law of October 20, 2020, which came into effect on January 1, 2021, has affected the minimum wage requirements, which determine the minimum value of work based on the degree of difficulty of the work. This change year after year reduces the differences in the value of work performed. In this context, it is necessary to update the characteristics and criteria for determining the degree of difficulty of work, to improve and clearly define their application in practice, to improve the system of controls and corrective measures, and to strengthen the labor inspectorate. The trade union also demands a legislative change that will allow minimum wage requirements to be respected by employers who have a valid collective agreement.

E. Addressing the Gender Pay Gap

A long-standing issue that remains unresolved is the gender pay gap, which has not shown any improvement. A priority must be to strengthen the position of women in the labor market, not only through tax incentives, but primarily through real wage increases, which will subsequently affect the basis for pension contributions. The existence of the gender pay gap is a result of a combination of factors stemming from societal stereotypes about the roles of women, education and upbringing (promoting stereotypes), the existence of wage and career "glass ceilings," motherhood as a disadvantage, horizontal and vertical segregation, and so on. The solution involves a mix of measures, such as:

*   Facilitating the earlier return of mothers to the labor market through the provision of sufficient childcare facilities, even for the youngest children.
*   Developing childcare infrastructure.
*   Providing accessible social and healthcare services for the elderly.
*   Allowing for more flexible working arrangements (e.g., shorter working hours) when needed by employees, with appropriate compensation.
*   Taking into account wage growth during maternity leave when calculating the woman's salary upon her return from maternity leave (an example of good practice is the indexation of salary scales in the public sector, which also translates into a new salary upon return from maternity leave through placement in a specific salary scale and grade).

In the near future, it will be necessary to implement Directive (EU) 2023/970 of May 10, 2023, of the European Parliament and of the Council, which strengthens the application of the principle of equal pay for men and women for equal work or work of equal value through transparency in remuneration and enforcement mechanisms. In the process of adoption and implementation, we require the active participation of social partners and close cooperation and communication with trade unions. Trade unions will hold expert discussions on further solutions.

F. Strengthening Collective Bargaining

We expect full engagement and respect for the recommendations of social partners in strengthening collective bargaining, so that the goals of the EU directive on adequate minimum wages in terms of gradually increasing the coverage of collective agreements are achieved. In accordance with the directive, social partners should participate in the creation and ongoing evaluation of a so-called Action Plan, which should guarantee a gradual increase in the coverage of employees by collective agreements to a level of 80% or more. The trade union demands:

*   Ensuring a favorable environment for bipartite and tripartite social dialogue, including collective bargaining, in the public and private sectors at all levels, including sectoral, regional, or enterprise levels.
*   Ensuring that workers and their representatives, including those who are members or representatives of trade unions, are protected from any measures that could harm them or have a negative impact on their employment when exercising their right to collective bargaining.
*   Promoting trust in social partners and supporting the conclusion of collective agreements. In the event of disagreement, and without prejudice to the right to access appropriate administrative and judicial procedures to enforce rights and obligations arising from law or collective agreements, the trade union should encourage and support mechanisms to resolve them.
*   Ensuring that collective bargaining and social dialogue (including institutionalized dialogue) are possible at all appropriate levels, including enterprise, sectoral, regional, or national levels, and supporting the coordination or interconnection between these levels.
*   Promoting greater coverage by collective bargaining and collective agreements and enabling effective collective bargaining.
*   Initiating legislative changes regarding the process of collective bargaining, excluding the application of the Civil Code to the process of concluding collective agreements.
*   Restoring the process of extending the validity of collective agreements of a higher level (1).
*   Within the preparation of the so-called Action Plan in the context of the European directive on adequate minimum wages, incorporating proposals and suggestions from the trade union (e.g., the Law on Collective Bargaining) into the process of creating, controlling, and subsequently amending legislation.

G. Remuneration in the Public Sector

Remuneration in the public sector is constantly hampered by the problem of low salary scales and the fact that some salary scales fall below the minimum wage. A comprehensive reform of remuneration in the public sector is therefore necessary, promoting wage growth, reflecting the growth of the minimum wage and the average wage in the national economy.

3. REDUCING WORKING HOURS

In Slovakia, people work significantly more hours than in other European countries. Digitalization, automation, climate change, and, more recently, the pandemic, are likely to change the century-old "tradition" of the eight-hour workday. Several countries and companies are already experimenting with reducing working hours, or even introducing a four-day workweek.

The need for a public discussion and support for reducing working hours is crucial. Priorities:
* Reducing the legally mandated weekly working hours without reducing wages.
* Reducing the number of hours worked, particularly by reducing hours worked during non-standard working hours (such as overtime, night shifts, and weekend/holiday work).
Slovakia currently holds an unfavorable position in Europe for the amount of work performed during night shifts, shifts, and non-standard working hours, including evenings, weekends, and holidays. Therefore, public policies should address ways to reduce the number of hours worked during these times. In this context, it is necessary to focus on reducing overtime work, reducing the number of hours of night work and work performed during non-standard hours, increasing vacation time, and expanding the number of paid sick days.
* Reducing the weekly working hours.
Another option is to reduce the number of hours in the working week. For example, some companies, including those in the public sector, already apply a 37.5-hour work week through collective agreements. Therefore, an alternative would be to reduce this to 35 hours, which would mean a 7-hour workday. 4. THE FUTURE OF WORK IN THE CONTEXT OF TRADITIONAL APPROACHES, BUT ALSO DIGITALIZATION, DEMOGRAPHIC DEVELOPMENT, AND CLIMATE CHANGE.
Priorities:
* Equality of opportunity for all and support for diversity.
Equality of opportunity in the workplace is enshrined in Slovak law, but there is still a lack of measures to support it in practice. Inequality most often affects disadvantaged individuals, women, and older people in the labor market, which is not always due to an attempt to circumvent the law, but often due to objective reasons. Reduced opportunities for certain groups of people lead to widening gender gaps, income poverty, and social exclusion. All of these impacts have been exacerbated by the pandemic. Given the aging population of Slovakia, measures should be taken to ensure intergenerational solidarity, such as the adoption of effective age management and measures in the area of active aging, as well as other measures aimed at strengthening the silver economy.
* Balancing work and family life.
Balancing work and family life can be achieved by increasing the availability of preschool facilities (providing childcare for parents working non-standard hours, both through preschool facilities and through individual arrangements), as well as supporting care for the elderly and social services, reducing working hours, and more.
* Employing older people and developing the silver economy.
Measures aimed at employing older people and supporting the development of the silver economy (adopting effective age management and measures in the area of active aging). This is directly related to the importance of increasing healthy life expectancy so that older people can remain in the labor market and enabling their participation in working life by creating favorable and individual working conditions. It will only be possible to keep older people employed for longer by educating them and maintaining their skills at the required level, for example, primarily digital skills. Combating Violence and Harassment in the Workplace The trade union demands the ratification of ILO Convention No. 190 of 2019 on violence and harassment, its subsequent implementation into national legislation, and explicit legal regulation of mobbing, bullying, harassment, and abuse in the workplace, as well as the establishment of tools for actively preventing all forms of harassment and violence in the world of work. Changes in Employment Policies Currently, the long-term unemployed are disproportionately represented by individuals with low levels of education, and they remain on unemployment rolls for longer than average. This trend appears to be persistent, despite existing employment policies. These policies need to be re-evaluated and shifted from supporting job creation to supporting education, especially in the context of an aging population and a shortage of skilled labor. In this regard, employment policies have been strengthened in recent periods, but primarily focused on national projects that are effective and beneficial, but financially unsustainable because they are linked to European funding. It is essential to establish sustainable employment policies that can function from public funds even after the end of the program period. Furthermore, support for the establishment of businesses has been strengthened in recent periods, as this tool has been utilized. However, given that more than half of the economic entities in Slovakia are sole proprietorships, further strengthening and support for their creation seems excessive. Moreover, Slovakia is not effectively combating fictitious "forced" sole proprietorships, where employees perform dependent work but are registered as self-employed. According to a recent analysis by the Slovak Audit Office (NKÚ), the number of fictitious sole proprietors in Slovakia has increased from 84,000 to nearly 110,000 over the past ten years. This results in a significantly lower tax and social security burden compared to the same work performed by an employee. Sole proprietors pay 6 times less in tax and social security contributions in the first year of business and 2.3 times less in subsequent years. In 2017, the flat-rate expenses were also increased from 40% to 60% of their earned income. Slovakia is beginning to grapple with unfavorable demographic trends. This is also beginning to manifest in a shortage of labor. At the same time, climate change may cause migration and also worsen working conditions in Slovakia. For these reasons, it will be necessary to approach migration policies with sensitivity, taking into account regulated and, to the greatest extent possible, controlled migration of labor, which must not create social, economic, or political tensions. Slovakia is still predominantly an industrial country, which affects the nature of work, the quality of the working environment, the health of employees, and the length of healthy life expectancy. SOCIAL POLICIES, INCLUDING PENSION ISSUES. Changes are needed in the area of social policies to effectively combat income poverty and material deprivation. Changes to the minimum income level and related benefits are an integral part of this. Priorities: E Adjustments to the amount, structure, and indexation of the minimum income level. In systemic measures aimed at addressing poverty and social exclusion, one of the tools is the minimum income level. Currently, it is set at a very low level in Slovakia. A one-time increase in its amount is necessary, followed by the definition of a new indexation mechanism to truly reflect real economic indicators and the needs of the intended recipients. In relation to the minimum income level, it will be necessary to focus on: - The fact that the indexation of the minimum income level does not reflect the reality that, until recently, was based on the growth of net incomes and the cost of living for households. With this indexation mechanism, where the minimum income level is indexed by a lower coefficient (either the coefficient of growth of net cash income per person or the coefficient of growth of the cost of living for low-income households, currently only inflation for low-income households), beneficiaries of state social benefits are trapped in a cycle of poverty. We recommend establishing a completely different mechanism. A large number of state social benefits and measures are linked to the minimum income level, and this method of indexation may disadvantage the poorest, as the growth of the cost of living for low-income households, and the indexation based on it, may be lower in some years than the indexation based on the consumer price index. - A one-time increase in the current level of the minimum income (re-setting the amounts of the minimum income) and subsequently establishing a new indexation mechanism. One option is to accurately determine the minimum income based on a minimum basket of goods and services. The administrative method of determining the level of minimum income (currently) should be raised to a higher qualitative level that reflects the development of other socio-economic variables and indicators and should be re-evaluated periodically (every 3-5 years). The government can decide whether to choose from the category of "wages" or "basket of goods" as the variables to be considered. - In relation to the categories of work, consider the relationship to the minimum and/or average wage. In this selection, the indexation of the minimum income would then reflect the growth of the minimum/average wage. (For example, in the Netherlands, limits are set for benefits for different types of households based on the number of members as a certain percentage of the minimum wage.) If the minimum income level were to rise to almost 50% of the gross minimum wage, employees earning the minimum wage would not pay taxes. This is because, among other things, the non-taxable portion of the tax base is linked to the minimum income level (expressed as 21 times its value). This would certainly help low-income employees. Overall, taxes would be reduced for employees, and net income would increase. - Consider two categories of minimum income: a lower level in the form of an existence minimum, taking into account basic needs, and a higher level in the form of a standard minimum, taking into account needs for culture, education, travel, recreation, etc. - Overhaul the entire system of benefits, subsidies, and tax implications linked to the minimum income level.


In European countries, systems for determining the minimum standard of living vary (including the terminology used, the methods of calculation, growth rates, and the factors considered, such as economic activity and household structure).

Regarding changes to unemployment benefits, the trade union recommends, in light of a reassessment of the concept of the minimum standard of living, that unemployment insurance be adjusted. One option is to shorten the duration of unemployment benefits for employees in older age groups and those nearing retirement, as they are the most vulnerable group in the labor market. An example from the Czech Republic shows that unemployment benefits can be differentiated by age, for example, for individuals aged 50-55, the benefit lasts for 8 months, and for those over 55, it lasts for up to 11 months. Furthermore, the current system of providing unemployment benefits is not flexible, does not reflect various life situations, and is fundamentally unfair, especially to employees who have not received benefits for a long time and who, for example, find themselves unemployed towards the end of their working lives.

Regarding changes to benefits for material need, despite several adjustments to benefits for material need, we still consider the current level and amount to be inadequate. We have consistently pointed out that the difference between the amount of the benefit, which is intended to be a safety net, and the minimum standard of living (i.e., the minimum necessary for survival) is significant. The benefit for material need does not even cover the essential costs of maintaining human dignity and perpetuates the cycle of material poverty and social exclusion.

**Rental housing:**
The current options for housing are based on a commercial principle, with excessively high rents and inadequate living conditions. It is desirable to have a law that would help regulate the profitable business practices that exploit people in housing need and would ensure housing with reasonable costs for the most vulnerable groups of the population. **Reform of the pension system:**
The trade union advocates for a pension system that provides an adequate level of pensions, allows for retirement based on the physical and mental demands of the work and the years worked, and guarantees a dignified and active life in old age, in the event of disability, and in difficult social situations. It is also necessary to take into account three main trends that will need to be addressed in the coming years and decades: the green economy, digitalization, and an aging population. It is also important to mention the current crises that are complicating changes to the pension system to improve it (the consequences of the COVID-19 pandemic, the war in Ukraine, or the energy crisis in Europe).

The trade union proposes, for example:
*   Increasing the current pension value. The previous government made non-systematic adjustments to the increase in the current pension value based on 95% of wage growth.
*   A pension after a standard number of years of work. A more favorable option for early retirement after 40 years of work has been introduced, but achieving this threshold is challenging. Therefore, it is appropriate to link this threshold to the expected life expectancy.
*   Adjusting the contribution rates to reflect the reality of financing the various components of the system.
*   We recommend, in support of parenthood, awarding additional salary points during the upbringing of children, which is a more stable alternative to the introduced parental pension.
*   Unifying newly recognized and continuing pensions.
*   Making participation in the third pillar more attractive, for example, by increasing the tax-deductible amount for savers: increasing the tax benefit for the employer if they contribute to the employee's pension savings, or introducing a state premium for voluntary pension savings, thereby increasing citizens' interest in pension savings.
*   Introducing the concept of a pre-retirement program.

6. ECONOMIC MODEL, PUBLIC FINANCES, AND TAXATION
The one-sided focus on industry and its concentration in only certain regions of Slovakia creates economic fragility in the country, but also generates significant environmental burdens. From this perspective, it is necessary to implement policies aimed at greater diversification of industry, which has long been a recognized need, but which is not reflected in strategic plans and planned policies. Investments should be directed towards sustainable production outside the automotive industry, and in addition to attracting foreign investors, support should be provided for the development of domestic companies. New investments should be more diversified into other sectors of industrial production, and not only into automotive manufacturing, which is more difficult to sustain in the long term, and whose products create significant environmental burdens throughout their entire life cycle and beyond.
Priorities:
E Improving the business environment without harming employees.
In the context of improving the business environment, the trade union believes that a systemic shift in economic policy is necessary to transition to a knowledge-based, socially and environmentally sustainable economy, which it considers a fundamental prerequisite for its transformation, sustainability, and prosperity in the future. The trade union has long supported improving the business environment through reducing excessive bureaucracy, streamlining the operations of public bodies, digitizing the public sector, and connecting the various systems of public sector bodies, as well as improving the enforcement of law, combating corruption, and tax evasion. We fundamentally reject improving the business environment at the expense of employees, decent jobs, reducing health and safety protections, or lowering wage levels. Furthermore, we state that measures to improve the business environment, and the related promotion of the interests of the private sector, must not override the general public interest, safety, health protection, environmental protection, consumer rights, etc. In this regard, the trade union supports, for example:
- Investments in a diverse range of industrial products.
- Strengthening the service and social sectors.
- Private investment in fixed capital (introduction of new technologies).
- Completing the infrastructure.
- Reducing excessive bureaucracy and red tape for employers, but without negatively impacting employees.
- Streamlining the operations of public bodies, digitizing the public sector, and connecting the various systems of public sector bodies.
- Combating corruption and tax evasion.
E Healthy public finances must not "burden" employees.
Recognizing the need to improve public finances, we advocate for a gradual consolidation that does not stifle the economy and does not cause a recession. At the same time, we reject consolidation that is solely at the expense of employees and socially vulnerable individuals. When discussing the draft of the public administration budget, the trade union expressed fundamental disagreement with the budget as a whole, particularly with the emphasis on reducing the number of employees, which, according to the draft, would also jeopardize the functioning of the state itself.
[FA PAGE 13 | PRIORITIES - Trade Union]
Analyses that have been carried out to date (e.g., the Review of Expenditures and Employment in the Public Sector - IFP) do not indicate that there is overemployment in the public sector in Slovakia, even when compared to other countries. Any blanket reduction in the number of employees would only exacerbate the shortage. Any changes in employment must be the result of natural changes caused by reforms, streamlining of processes, reducing bureaucracy, and natural retirement.
E Changing the tax mix - a tool for fair taxation and sustainable public finances.
The trade union considers changing the tax mix to be the basis for fair taxation, emphasizing that taxation must be progressive, lead to a reduction in the burden on labor and an increase in the burden on capital, and include reforms associated with the introduction of new taxes that tax new forms of business that have previously avoided responsibility (e.g., the introduction of a digital tax). The performance of the economy, or the increase in prices, which naturally increases the volume of tax revenues, also significantly contributes to increasing the revenues of the public sector. It would be appropriate to reduce the tax burden on labor, but it is also necessary to state that the overall burden is around the EU average. The share of the burden on the employer's side (in the case of Slovakia, on contributions) is what reaches above-average levels and generally increases the burden. Any shortfalls in taxes and contributions (labor burden) must be compensated, given the impact of this shortfall on the social and health sectors and local governments (e.g., subsidies for health insurance from the state budget).

Increasing the tax burden on economically inactive individuals – the budget must increase revenue, either through tax increases or reduced spending.  Furthermore, the Social Security Fund's debt must be covered from the state budget, and there needs to be compensation for the loss of revenue for local governments. Any changes to taxes and levies on employment require further systemic changes in the financing of social security and health insurance, as these are currently in deficit, and also require compensation for the loss of revenue for local governments. Given that the overall tax burden is comparable to the EU average, changes in the tax mix could put pressure on employee contributions to ensure that the burden is distributed between employers and employees in a way that is comparable to the EU average. Therefore, any reform of the tax burden on employment must be carried out in consultation with social partners, and ideally, the tax burden on employment should be reduced in favor of other taxes (such as taxes on negative externalities, such as taxes on vehicles based on CO2 emissions, or taxes on outdoor advertising), improved tax collection, and new taxes.  The tax burden on low-income earners should be reduced, for example, through adjustments to the non-taxable income threshold (hereinafter referred to as "NCT").  Due to the fact that the NCT is linked to the minimum income level, which has not changed since 2013 despite rising wages, net wages have grown more slowly than gross wages, which has had a negative impact on household incomes and living standards.  While in 2013, even those earning the minimum wage were "exempt" from income tax thanks to a sufficiently high NCT, they were required to pay income tax due to the stagnant minimum income level.  The NCT should not be adjusted based on rising prices or living costs, as its function is to maintain the progressivity of the tax system, ensuring that low-income individuals pay a lower proportion of their income in taxes than high-income individuals.  Therefore, the NCT is determined differently depending on the amount of the tax base.  A systemic solution would be to link the NCT to the minimum wage or to set it as a certain percentage of the average wage in the national economy, taking into account wage indicators and maintaining its function.  This would increase the amount of the NCT and increase the net wage for employees.  This measure is considered necessary, and its proper implementation would help low-income households.  Furthermore, the NCT would change in line with changes in wages in the national economy.  When increasing a particular tax, it is advisable to focus on those that make up the largest proportion, and recommendations primarily focus on increasing revenue through indirect taxes.  It is primarily important to increase/improve the collection of the established tax.  Further revenue can be found by increasing excise taxes (on alcohol and tobacco), particularly by harmonizing or increasing taxes on alcoholic beverages.  The overall tax burden on capital is low compared to EU countries, but this is not offset by the tax burden on corporate income, which is among the highest in the EU and above the EU and Eurozone average.  The tax burden on households, including self-employed individuals and taxes on capital assets, which include property tax, capital taxes including inheritance tax, property tax, and taxes on the use of fixed assets, is also a concern.  Furthermore, there may be professional and business licenses, and some product taxes, as well as other taxes and levies that may be considered necessary to start production.  The low tax burden on income for self-employed individuals and sole proprietors is a problem, as they represent a relatively high number of entities with a significantly lower tax burden.  Furthermore, they often receive significant compensation in the form of state social benefits (e.g., minimum pensions) during their post-productive years.  The property tax is also at a low level.  These taxes (and the aforementioned capital taxes) should be included in the change in the tax mix and compensate for the reduction in the tax burden on employment.  The characteristics of Slovakia in terms of real estate (a high proportion of real estate owned by households) mean that any increase in property taxes must be approached with caution and take these specific factors into account.  When making changes to property taxes, especially the property tax, it is considered most important that the determination of these taxes should be preceded by the creation of price maps, and that increased taxes should not negatively affect the costs for households that own only one property, which is also subject to a mortgage.  The property tax is a source of revenue for local governments, so an increase in this tax can compensate for the loss of revenue from income tax for low-income earners.  The union also supports a discussion on the introduction of new taxes, such as considering the introduction of an inheritance tax that does not burden ordinary heirs, restoring the tax on gifts and transfers of real estate.  New environmental taxes, such as a carbon tax or a tax on plastics, should also be considered.

Given the impact of digitalization on the world of work, public finances, and the content and nature of public policies, the trade union considers it appropriate to initiate a serious discussion about identifying additional sources of funding that would guarantee the implementation of public policies and the provision of public services. The trade union will propose and promote changes aimed at what is known as "taxes and levies on robots and automation."
