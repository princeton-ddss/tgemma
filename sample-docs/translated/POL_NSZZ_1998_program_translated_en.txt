RESOLUTION: PROGRAMMATIC DECLARATION OF THE "UNITY" TRADE UNION. The Independent Self-Governing Trade Union "Unity" was founded in 1980 to peacefully defend the interests of workers and their families, and guided by Catholic social teaching, it seeks to build social relations based on truth, respect for human dignity, freedom, and patriotism. Our vision for the state is outlined in the Citizens' Constitution Project and the "Unity" electoral program. The victory of the AWS (Agricultural and Rural Solidarity) in the parliamentary elections created an opportunity to fully implement this program. The four-year term ahead presents "Unity" with new and challenging tasks. The fate of Polish workers is increasingly dependent on the development of the international situation and the globalization of the world economy. Poland, as a member of the OECD and in negotiations to join the European Union, is integrating into its structures and becoming subject to its rules. The situation in the country is undergoing fundamental changes due to the implemented systemic reforms: changes in the administrative structure and decentralization of the state, reforms in education, social security, healthcare, and the introduction of health insurance. The economic transformation is continuing, characterized by a reduction in the role of traditional sectors, especially heavy industry, due to restructuring, the development of trade and services, and an increase in private ownership. The result of these changes is not only rapid economic growth and technological and organizational progress, but also persistent unemployment, increasing social inequality, deregulation of labor relations, and declining union membership. These conditions require a redefinition of the role of the trade union in economic and social policy, in relations with new partners in public life and the labor market. Social Policy The tasks outlined in the programmatic resolution of the "Unity" trade union – increasing wages, reducing unemployment, ensuring universal access to medical services and education, protecting families from poverty, and providing social security benefits – remain relevant. In the changed socio-political and economic situation, the methods and means of achieving these goals require re-evaluation. The implemented reforms require the trade union to negotiate effective social safety nets for workers who are being laid off. 1. Increasing Real Wages The trade union will strive to: 2. Ensure real wage growth in line with the country's economic growth, eliminate disparities between wages in the public sector and wages in the business sector, and change the methodology for determining the minimum wage. Combating Unemployment We will demand a pro-employment policy from the state, coordination of employment programs, increased competence of employment agencies, and improved service for the unemployed. It is particularly important to: 3. Introduce a mandatory unemployment insurance system, strengthen earmarked funds such as the Labor Fund, the Fund for Guaranteed Employee Benefits, and the State Fund for the Rehabilitation of Disabled Persons, to support employment, legally regulate continuing education for workers, ratify ILO conventions: No. 102 on social security, No. 1500 on labor administration, No. 168 on promoting employment, and No. 173 on protecting employee claims. Social Security, Protecting Families from Poverty The following require urgent implementation: - Establishing a legal definition for the concepts of "minimum social level" and "minimum subsistence level," - Developing and implementing a schedule for introducing guarantees for the most vulnerable groups, - Determining the cost of living for pensioners and retirees to change the rules for adjusting benefits, especially the lowest ones, - Legally regulating opportunities to equalize the chances of people with disabilities, guaranteeing, among other things, the right to rehabilitation, education, and vocational and social reintegration; the realization of these rights must take place at all levels, from central to local, - Continuing work on a system of protective measures related to the liberalization of energy prices, - Introducing protection against eviction for poor and elderly people. 4. Social Security The ongoing pension reform means that the most important tasks are: - Introducing legislative solutions prepared through negotiations for people employed in special conditions who, due to the nature of their work, are unable to work until the generally applicable retirement age, - Monitoring the implementation of the new system, - Protecting the acquired rights of current pensioners and retirees. 5. Social Dialogue We will develop a tripartite social dialogue. We believe that the basis is the legal definition of the principles of its functioning and the criteria for the representativeness of the partners.

We will strive to prioritize negotiations at the enterprise, industry, and regional levels in shaping collective labor relations. We believe it is essential to expand the scope of collective agreements to cover all enterprises within specific sectors. A union working group on collective labor agreements should be established. Social and political reforms within the state lead to the transfer of new tasks to local government structures and the creation of new levels of government, which means that the union will have new partners with whom to negotiate collective labor agreements. This requires strengthening these structures at the regional and enterprise (inter-enterprise) levels, as their role will increase due to their participation in shaping collective labor relations. In light of the state's administrative reform, the union should negotiate guarantees for workers in various industries.

6.  Healthcare: We will strive to fully realize citizens' rights to healthcare. This involves introducing legislative changes to the universal health insurance law, restoring the basic principles of the "social security" and AWS (social insurance) programs. The union should prepare qualified representatives to serve on health insurance fund boards to effectively represent the interests of insured individuals and monitor the progress of the reform. As part of our ongoing efforts to improve the healthcare system, the union will seek to increase contributions to health insurance funds to ensure the provision of medical services in accordance with legal requirements. We will strive to complete negotiations on a sectoral, inter-enterprise collective labor agreement and extend it to all healthcare facilities. Changes in payment methods and reductions in employment must lead to improvements in the wage system and salary levels.

7.  Education, Science, and Culture: We will ensure that the reform of education, science, and culture proceeds in accordance with the principles of "social security." We will particularly advocate for:
    *   Guaranteeing equal access to education, cultural resources, and scientific advancements for all citizens.
    *   Significantly improving the material conditions of workers in these sectors, especially teachers, who bear the brunt of the education reform.
    *   Adjusting the level of funding for these sectors to match the constitutional obligations of the state.
    *   Promoting culture through state patronage and introducing legislation that fosters its development in other forms.

8.  Worker Safety: One of the primary tasks of the union during the 1998-2002 term will be to reform the worker safety system in Poland. The current system is characterized by a lack of coordination among its various entities, often a blurring of responsibilities, and the absence of certain elements. Key elements of the reformed system include:
    1.  Legally binding regulations that align our safety and health standards with the requirements of European Union regulations.
    2.  Economic incentives that encourage the creation of good working conditions, including differentiated insurance premiums based on the risk category of the workplace.
    3.  Oversight of the operation of the worker safety system in Poland at all levels (national, regional, voivodeship, and enterprise), exercised by representatives of the government, employers, and employees.
    4.  Reforming the appointment process, composition, and powers of the National Labor Inspectorate to ensure its tripartite character.
    5.  Improving the health of workers, which is currently a source of concern, and conducting assessments of workers' exposure to harmful or stressful factors in the workplace.
    6.  Improving methods for preventing workplace accidents and enhancing training in safety and health.

Economic Policy: The union's policy regarding employee participation in privatization processes remains relevant, as it is essential for effectively protecting and defending workers' rights. Continued employee participation in management through active involvement in supervisory boards and influencing the appointment of employee affairs managers in privatized companies is crucial. Legislation should be introduced to enable the participation of employee representatives in the governing and supervisory bodies of companies operating in the Polish market. We believe that the state must wage a decisive fight against corruption, especially among public officials. We believe it is appropriate to establish an independent office of a public appointments commissioner.

1.  Industrial Policy: In order to protect existing jobs and create new ones, the union will strive to influence the state's industrial policy.

We believe that this policy should be based on:
- government and local government support for industries and sectors undergoing restructuring,
- support for research and development in the face of increased competition for Polish companies in connection with the planned accession to the EU,
- promoting innovation, entrepreneurship, and human initiative, productivity, and adaptability of workers in a competitive labor market,
- combating monopolistic practices and unfair competition, including foreign competition,
- reducing pollution by introducing new, environmentally friendly technologies.
We will strive to equalize the rights of former state-owned enterprise (PGR) employees with the rights of employees in privatized industrial enterprises.

1. Regional Policy
We will advocate for the adoption of a law on state regional policy. We recognize that the creation of regional economic development programs and a state equalization policy for weaker regions are essential for the cohesion and sustainable development of the state. The result of these efforts, especially the expansion of infrastructure and direct investment, should be increased employment.

2. Promoting Ownership Through Social Empowerment
The union has repeatedly and strongly advocated for the widespread empowerment of citizens and has not changed its position on this matter. We believe that empowering citizens is a fundamental condition for stimulating economic activity, democratizing the economy, and ensuring stable economic development. The union has developed a number of draft laws in this area and has formulated the basic principles of an empowerment program. The union remains committed to the AWS's pre-election commitment to implement empowerment and that this commitment must be enshrined in law by 1998. It is the duty of AWS members of parliament to ensure that parliament adopts, still this year, a package of empowerment laws prepared by AWS members of parliament, especially the draft law on the principles of implementing a program of universal empowerment of citizens of the Republic of Poland, concerning direct and indirect empowerment. The union, supporting privatization and social security reform, believes that they must be implemented in harmony with the empowerment program, not in opposition to it.

3. Tax Policy
Tax policy should not only support economic development but should also be family-friendly. Building tax incentives should be maintained to facilitate the development of housing construction. One of the union's primary goals is to significantly reduce direct taxes, which should ultimately lead to increased prosperity for citizens. In the area of indirect taxes, we advocate for their significant simplification. The union considers it socially justified to maintain a progressive income tax.

Internal Union Affairs
1. Strengthening and Developing the Union
The number of union members reflects the support it enjoys among workers and, to a large extent, determines its importance and the size of its material resources. As experience in recent years has shown, identifying with the union's ideological declaration is no longer a sufficient reason for workers to join the union. In view of the decline in union membership, the National Executive Committee is committed to analyzing the causes of this phenomenon and preparing an action plan to halt this process and allocating more financial resources to the implementation of specific tasks. In particular, it is necessary to assess how to utilize existing experience and what is most needed to attract new members to existing union organizations in workplaces. It is also necessary to determine in which environments the union's proposals have the greatest chance of success. A priority should be given to the development of union organizations in private companies, especially small and medium-sized enterprises (the possibility of introducing the function of a union representative). We will improve the information system, using modern technology at all levels of the union's territorial and industry structures. We should consider the possibility of creating an alternative method of collecting union dues, which would promote increased membership. Given that the union's position on the international stage also depends on the participation of its structures in international and European industry federations, the union will strive to prepare union activists for work in the structures of European Works Councils and international institutions of social dialogue.

2. Adapting the Union's Structure to the New Administrative Division and Economic Situation
The union calls on its regional structures to cooperate and establish a joint union representation with the new public authorities (voivodeships and counties).

The union should prepare a plan to adapt the structure of the union – both regional and industry-specific – to the new administrative division of the country, the changing economic situation, and changes in competencies. The implementation of the local government reform will make local government bodies a significant employer. It is necessary to amend the law on trade unions, which will strengthen the role of regional and industry-specific union structures as a social partner with all levels of local government. 3. Strengthening the financial stability of the union should strive to increase revenue from membership fees (through improved collection and increased membership) and develop an optimal model for the distribution of membership fees among the structures of the union. It is also necessary to seek other sources of funding for union activities, such as from asset recovery and business activities. This financial strengthening is essential to maintain and improve the strength of the union, including through ongoing training for union activists and providing expert support (e.g., through consultation and negotiation departments and experts preparing commissioned studies). 4. Union training The union should develop training programs for activists and members, preparing them for active and competent participation in new economic and social conditions. In particular, training should be provided in: a) knowledge of how businesses operate, b) the protection of workers' rights (negotiating collective agreements and social packages), c) for members of supervisory boards of various types of public and insurance institutions, d) on how to recruit new members. International cooperation International cooperation is of particular importance in the face of the globalization of the world economy and the start of negotiations on Poland's accession to the European Union. The global offensive of capital requires a global response from trade unions. The gaps in the world's union landscape must be filled. We must counter the situation where multinational companies, taking advantage of areas where there are no strong, independent, and democratic trade unions, move production and jobs there, offering workers working conditions and wages below all acceptable standards. This not only leads to the inhumane exploitation of labor, often of children, but also creates unfair competition and leads to increased unemployment or lower wages in other regions. Therefore, we will continue to support the activities of the International Confederation of Free Trade Unions (ICFTU) and the World Federation of Trade Unions (WFTU) in promoting respect for human rights, workers' rights, and trade union rights in all countries, based on the standards of the International Labour Organization, in eliminating forced labor and child labor, in integrating the informal sector into the formal economy, and in promoting trade and investment liberalization that takes into account social dimensions and is accompanied by sustainable economic development and job creation. We will support the creation and development of independent and democratic trade unions, which is not only an expression of our solidarity with workers in other countries but also serves the vital interests of Polish workers. We welcome the initiatives to create a joint union front between the ICFTU and the WFTU; we will continue to support such initiatives from both sides, which could lead to unity of action among trade unions and, in the future, perhaps to the unification of both international confederations. We believe that the joint action of independent, democratic, and representative trade unions within a single international confederation, while respecting the rights and identity of each member organization, would avoid unnecessary competition and dispersion of forces and would contribute to greater effectiveness of international union action. The threats arising from the efforts of multinational companies and foreign investors to maximize profits by reducing labor costs through layoffs, low wages, disregard for health and safety standards and environmental regulations, and by forcing maximum employee availability through flexible employment contracts and collective agreements, are also very strong in our country. We will oppose these phenomena with all our might, both through joint actions with friendly trade union organizations abroad on a bilateral basis and through participation in international confederation campaigns. In this context, the union's affiliation this year to the Trade Union Advisory Committee (TUAC) of the OECD and the activities of this organization to negotiate a multilateral agreement on investment (MAI) and to review the OECD's guidelines for multinational companies are of particular importance. The union will continue to actively participate in the work of the structures of the ICFTU, WFTU, TUAC, and the ILO.

We will actively participate in the expert work of the TUAC. The International Department will continue to organize, through international organizations and on a bilateral basis, the exchange of experiences, information, and expertise, according to the needs of the unions and relevant departments, and will support the activities of similar structures within the union. International cooperation in the field of training will also continue. European integration presents enormous challenges for the union, not only within the country but also in international policy, especially in light of the upcoming negotiations regarding our country's integration with the European Union. After regaining the ability to sovereignly decide its own destiny, the Polish nation not only has the right to claim its rightful place within the family of European nations, but also the obligation to participate in shaping the face of a free Europe. European integration offers Poland the opportunity to ensure security, strengthen democracy, and accelerate the achievement of prosperity for its society. The integration process promotes economic development and social progress, but also entails costs and risks. These should be carefully identified and minimized. The union, as a social partner, has the responsibility not only to participate in shaping the position of the Polish negotiating team, but also to represent the interests of its members to the institutions of the European Union, primarily through the European trade union movement. We will strive to increase the role of organizations from outside the European Economic Area, even during the pre-accession period. This is particularly important because, in the near future, a debate will be held and positions will be taken on specific issues related to the negotiations on the expansion of the European Union. Of particular importance are the transitional periods for full harmonization with European legislation, as well as the institutional reform of the European Union, which is directly linked to the expansion, and the reform of the common agricultural policy, and the expected restructuring of the EU budget that will follow. The Commission for European Integration within the union, with the participation of industry-specific structures, will provide analyses that will serve as a starting point for the union to make decisions, based on which the union's position will be represented. A very important issue for the union, which is expected to be the subject of difficult discussion during both the accession negotiations and within the organization, is the issue of the transitional period for the free movement of labor from and to the new member states. We believe that the free movement of workers must be implemented without any transitional period that could pose a threat to the very process of European integration, and therefore we will present a detailed argument on this issue. The implementation of the tasks outlined above will ensure that the reforms expected by society and deemed necessary will proceed in accordance with the principles of the union. We hope that their implementation will prevent a deterioration of the situation and, in the long term, create a lasting foundation for improving the material and social situation of Polish workers and their families. In this way, at the dawn of the 21st century, we will contribute to fulfilling the hopes and aspirations of the Polish people, especially the younger generation. In doing so, we will serve the well-understood interests of Poland.
