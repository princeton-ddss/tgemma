Introduction: The Work of Many Dedicated People

The work and achievements of the union are the result of the efforts of many dedicated individuals: within the union itself, in its branches, in companies, and in various institutions. Successes are achieved both on a small and large scale. This report describes the work of the past period, highlighting the union's key priorities and how each department and division has contributed to our collective successes – from "Lower Income Tax!", a successful membership recruitment campaign that brought the union its first increase in membership in a long time, to hundreds of collective bargaining agreements, legislative reviews, and ongoing internal modernization efforts. Whether it was the development of campaign content and priorities, their integration into union training programs, their communication and implementation, the provision of information and advice to members, works councils, and employee representatives, the organization of events, and, of course, the financing of all of this – every contribution is important. In addition, the report provides an overview of other key areas of the union's work since 2013. This includes laws that were enacted at the initiative of the union – for example, to promote fairer employment contracts, improve hardship assistance, and abolish tuition fees for apprentices. It also includes new information and service offerings for members of works councils and employee representation bodies, as well as information, education, and cultural initiatives.

Unacceptable Low Wages

Everyone who works full-time must be able to live on their salary. Full-time employees should not be forced to rely on additional social benefits to make ends meet. Furthermore, minimum wages should prevent a "race to the bottom" in wages. In early 2017, Chancellor Christian Kern presented his first core program – Plan A – and called on the collective bargaining partners to develop a phased plan to achieve a minimum wage of €1,500. The union had already set the goal of achieving higher minimum wages through collective bargaining agreements. In Austria, almost 98 percent of all employment relationships are covered by collective bargaining agreements that set minimum wages and salaries.

Better Through Collective Bargaining

A statutory minimum wage would be inferior to the system of collectively agreed minimum wages in many respects. The union has set itself the goal of achieving a "general agreement" among the social partners, similar to what was agreed in 2007, to gradually introduce a minimum wage of €1,000. In June 2017, the social partners agreed on a gross minimum wage of €1,500 for all employees, which will be implemented by 2020.

Next Step: €1,700 Gross

For the union, €1,500 gross, which was defined as a goal as early as 2013, is only an intermediate step. The new minimum wage target is €1,700 gross – a goal that the union will continue to demand and enforce.

Europe Needs Higher Wages

At the European level, there was also a campaign for higher minimum wages. Workers deserve a fair share of rising profits! To make this a reality, the European trade union movement launched the campaign "Europe Needs Higher Wages – Now is the Time for Growth" in 2017.

Working Hours

In parallel with the social partner negotiations on minimum wages, a reform of working time regulations was also discussed. The union's goal: greater flexibility that benefits workers, providing them with more predictability, autonomy, and reduced working hours. For decades, working hours have been a constant topic of discussion for the social partners, and since 1997, it has been completely undisputed that exceptions to the basic rule of an 8-hour day and a 40-hour week are necessary for companies to remain competitive – but also to allow workers to balance work, family, and personal life.

Flexibility Must Not Be a One-Way Street

Where employers want to use employees based on capacity requirements, workers need, in return, more autonomy, predictability, and reduced working hours. However, employers wanted to extend the normal working hours to ten hours per day and 50 hours per week without overtime. The maximum working hours, including overtime, should be twelve hours per day or 60 hours per week, with the possibility of calculating these hours over two or more years. This would have meant for employees: those who have to work overtime for two years would only be paid for those overtime hours after two years.

Unreasonable Demands from Businesses

The importance of the Working Time Act as a protective law, safeguarding employees from excessive physical and psychological strain, was completely disregarded. Therefore, it was clear that the trade union movement could not accept the unreasonable demands of businesses, even if it meant making small concessions to employees. Consequently, no negotiated solution was reached.

The Issue Remains on the Table

The issue of flexible working hours remains on the negotiating table for social partners. It plays a role in negotiations for collective agreements and company agreements. It is certain that it will be discussed again at the level of umbrella organizations. There is now sufficient basis for this after many intensive rounds of negotiations.

Education Summit

In 2016, the government agreed on an education package that provides schools with more autonomy. This includes allowing for flexible class and group sizes, as well as the duration of teaching units. Union: Administrative units are to become "school clusters," which can include up to eight schools. There should be more funding for all-day schools. What is missing: a "chances index," which would provide more funding for schools with many students from disadvantaged backgrounds.

Skilled Worker Scholarships

Since 2017, thanks to the efforts of the trade union and the Chamber of Labor, skilled worker scholarships have been available: People who no longer see a future in their current profession and want to pursue training in a profession that is in high demand in the labor market can receive financial support of approximately 800 euros per month. However, these scholarships are now threatened with being cut due to austerity measures by the federal government.

Free Boarding for Vocational School Students

Apprentices who live in a boarding school during their vocational school studies no longer have to pay for it. The insolvency wage fund will cover the costs from 2018. This followed a successful campaign by the trade union.

Apprentice Monitor

The trade union and the Chamber of Labor surveyed apprentices in Austria in 2015 and 2017. The results showed that problems for apprentices include the irregular presence of responsible trainers, a lack of feedback culture in companies, a poor working environment, unauthorized or involuntary overtime, and the cost of boarding.

Kindergartens

In 2015, social partners and the IV (Institute for Social Policy Research) agreed on a program for early childhood education. They are calling for all competencies to be consolidated at the federal level. They are also calling for a second mandatory kindergarten year, longer opening hours, and per capita funding. A federal framework law should regulate group sizes, preparation and follow-up times, and spatial requirements.

Vocational School Student Representative Initiative

Since 2015, the trade union has been involved in the area of vocational schools, promoting a strong and independent representation for vocational school students and supporting their work. In recent years, it has been possible to place several vocational school student representatives from the trade union.

Education for Young People Up to 18

Parents must ensure that young people up to the age of 18 either attend school, complete an apprenticeship, or pursue other training, such as AMS (Public Employment Service) courses or internships. Education up to the age of 18, and thus a qualification, protects young people from unemployment later in life and is of great importance.

Trade Union Campaigns

The trade union has achieved great success with campaigns such as "Eliminate Boarding Costs!" and "700 Euro Minimum Apprentice Allowance." "Europe, what now?", "Strong against violence," and "Check your Job" are further examples of trade union campaigns. In addition, there are annual Christmas and vacation pay campaigns, which have also helped to recruit many new members.

Collective Agreement Guide

As part of the National Action Plan for Equality, the trade union and the WKÖ (Austrian Federal Economic Chamber) have agreed on a guide. This promotes equality between men and women at the collective agreement level.

Work-Life Balance Package

The work-life balance package, adopted at the end of 2015, improved the work-life balance for employees and thus their situation in the labor market. It includes long-standing demands of the trade union: protection against dismissal after a miscarriage, parental leave for foster parents without the intention of adoption, and the right to information for part-time employees about full-time jobs offered by the company. The planned reduction in parental leave was prevented.

Income Reports

Since 2014, all companies with more than 150 employees have been required to create internal income reports.

Collective Bargaining Agreements: The number of union members and employees classified within specific collective bargaining groups must be specified, along with the average, adjusted-for-working hours, income for each group.

Childcare Allowance: The childcare allowance is becoming more flexible. This means that the previous four standard options are being replaced by a flexible childcare allowance account. The advantage is that the financial differences between the previous models are eliminated, and parents have access to a uniform total amount.

"Dad Month": Fathers who wish to spend the first few weeks with their spouse and child can agree with their employer on a "dad month" (family leave). In addition to the financial payment of €700, health and pension insurance remain in place.

Industrial Policy: In June 2015, the unions and the Chamber of Labor presented their positions on a new industrial policy to the government. They are calling for a master plan to strengthen Austria's economic position: maintaining and expanding energy, transportation, and communication infrastructure, providing excellent education and training, and implementing targeted technology and innovation policies.

Best Value, Not Just Cheapest: Public contracts should no longer be awarded solely based on the lowest price, but also consider social standards, quality, and long-term costs. A corresponding amendment to the Federal Procurement Act was unanimously approved by the National Council in 2015. In 2018, another reform of procurement law was adopted as a further step in the right direction.

After the Economic Crisis: The rescue and stimulus packages, largely funded by taxes paid by employees, saved the assets of a small elite. The unions have demanded that these individuals also contribute to the recovery. This has been partially implemented: a banking package, a stabilization levy, and advance payments were introduced. Following the bank rescues using public funds, it is possible to maintain banks in public ownership (e.g., Heta and KAFinanz).

Reduced Risk for Bank Customers: Banks are only allowed to offer products to their customers that are appropriate for their risk profile and that they understand (stricter prospectus requirements).

Strengthening the Fight Against Wage and Social Dumping: An amendment to the law on combating wage and social dumping, which came into force in 2015, provides for: not only checking whether employees receive the minimum wage, but also bonuses, bonuses, etc. Failure to provide wage documentation will be subject to stricter penalties. Penalties are applied per employee, not per company. Affected employees will be informed if they have received too little money so that they can claim their rights.

Law on Combating Social Fraud: Improved cooperation between authorities and faster enforcement measures can identify and punish shell companies. The focus is not only on companies that disappear from the market without paying taxes, social security contributions, and wages, but also on companies that create fictitious registrations and provide registered individuals with unjustified benefits. Furthermore, the liability of clients has been extended.

Record Unemployment: At the end of January 2015, the Austrian Public Employment Service (AMS) reported the highest unemployment rate in Austria since 1945. A total of 472,539 people were unemployed, and the national unemployment rate was 10.8 percent. Unemployment remained high for some time after that, and it was not until 2018 that the situation began to improve.

Emergency Assistance: From July 1, 2018, only the individual's own income will be used to calculate emergency assistance. The income of the partner will no longer be taken into account. The unions see this as an important step towards independence and poverty reduction.

Minimum Income Guarantee: The unions opposed the intensively discussed plans for cuts and called for a nationwide solution. The needs-based minimum income guarantee must remain the last safety net against poverty. The current discussion should not be used as an excuse or a pretext for social cuts.

Refugees: In view of the large number of refugees coming to (and through) Austria, the unions have repeatedly advocated for humanity and emphasized that the right to asylum must not be weakened. The unions are also committed to facilitating access to the labor market for refugees. More than 100,000 people attended a solidarity concert for refugees in the center of Vienna, where bands such as "Die toten Hosen" and Zucchero performed.

Annual Adjustment of Living Expenses Allowance: At the urging of the unions, the training allowance for apprentices in inter-company training – the so-called "coverage of living expenses" (DLU) – has been adjusted annually since 2014.

In addition, the minimum wage for apprentices over the age of 18 was significantly increased, so that they receive a monthly payment of €753 in 2018, regardless of the year of their apprenticeship. However, in 2018, the government decided to reduce this amount again. Employment contracts became fairer in 2016: all-inclusive contracts must now clearly state the base salary, which increases transparency and effectively eliminates all-inclusive contracts. Employers can only reclaim training costs from employees who change jobs for a maximum of four years, and the amount they can reclaim decreases with each month. Non-compete clauses are now only possible for employees who earn at least €3,240 gross per month. Employees have a legally enforceable right to receive a monthly salary statement. Equal treatment for workers and employees: In its last session before the 2017 elections, the National Council approved uniform regulations for workers and employees, including equal rules for continued payment during illness, uniform termination rules, and equal rights for taking time off for personal reasons. This is a milestone for the working world of the 21st century. Service interruption in the event of a disaster: The regulations for workers regarding service interruption for other important reasons cannot be amended by collective agreements in the event of a disaster. Information obligation: Part-time employees must be informed about available full-time positions in companies. Temporary employment: Temporary workers are treated the same as permanent employees of the employer company regarding company pension schemes. After the fifth year of temporary employment, they are entitled to a contribution/premium payment to the pension fund/company collective insurance. Distinction between self-employed and dependent workers: The regional health insurance fund, the SVA (for self-employed individuals), and the farmers' health insurance fund jointly verify whether individuals who register as self-employed are actually working independently. This will lead to greater legal certainty for insured individuals and clients, especially in industries that struggle with sham self-employment, such as the construction industry. The new regulations are based on an agreement between social partners. Reforms are having an effect: The actual retirement age is increasing. Studies show that the federal funds flowing into pensions will be significantly lower in the coming years than previously assumed. This confirms that the reforms implemented so far are having an effect. Pension account and pension calculator: Since 2014, the pension account has been in place. Workers and employees born in 1955 or later received information from the pension insurance company, including the amount of pension entitlements they have already earned. The pension account has made it easier to predict the future pension amount. Higher minimum pension for single individuals: Single pensioners who have worked for 30 years or more are entitled to a supplementary allowance of €1,000 (instead of €889.84). This encourages people to contribute to the system for a longer period of time, while also consistently continuing the principle of preventing poverty in old age. Improved rehabilitation: Since 2017, there has been a legal right to rehabilitation under certain conditions. Vocational rehabilitation is only possible with the consent of the individual. Part-time work after a long illness: Severely ill employees should have an easier time returning to work. After at least six weeks of sick leave, they can return to work with a reduction in working hours of 25 to 50 percent. In addition to their salary for the work performed, they receive a proportional sickness benefit. This can only be agreed upon on a voluntary basis. Expansion of ELGA: Hospitals in Styria and Vienna have been working with the electronic health record system ELGA since 2015, and the remaining federal states and accident insurance institutions (AUVA) joined in 2016. Further expansion phases include doctors' offices and pharmacies. Nursing training: Training reform for nurses: training in three groups, with advanced nursing specialists being trained academically at universities of applied sciences. Nursing leave and part-time nursing: Since 2014, it has been possible to take up to three months of nursing leave or part-time nursing leave in order to better organize the care of family members without having to give up a job for reasons of time. Employee protection: Employees in the EU are better protected from work-related cancers: introduction of binding occupational exposure limits (OELs) for eleven additional carcinogenic substances. In addition, member states must now organize lifelong health monitoring for employees who are exposed to carcinogens.

Union, Union. Because healthcare and social service providers are public, private, and non-profit, employees are organized in various unions. The FGV union represents their interests across different unions, for example, with a major inquiry into care in 2014, but also with demonstrations when necessary. The union operates on a similar principle. New Union Leadership. At the union congress in Paris in 2015, the European trade union movement elected its leadership team for the next five years, including Belgian Rudy De Leuuw (President) and Italian Luca Visentini (General Secretary). The delegates also advocated for a stronger youth guarantee and EU-wide minimum standards for corporate taxation. The next union congress will be held in Vienna in 2019. Social Pillar of the EU. In 2017, EU heads of state and government signed a declaration on the social rights of EU citizens. The so-called Social Pillar includes, among other things, the right to equal opportunities, fair working conditions, fair wages, and education. Unions criticize the non-binding nature of these measures. Social Progress Protocol. The union is calling for a legally binding Social Progress Protocol in the EU treaties. An initiative by the union, LO-Sweden, and social democratic politicians has been joined by unions and parties from many countries. The goal is to enshrine fundamental social rights in the EU treaties. Free Trade Agreements. The free trade agreement between the EU and Canada, CETA, was signed in October 2016. The union has consistently maintained its opposition to EU free trade agreements such as CETA and TTIP, and has also organized major demonstrations against them. Tax Avoidance. EU finance ministers have generally agreed to curb profit shifting by companies to avoid taxes. Starting in 2019, multinational corporations will be required to pay taxes in the countries where profits are generated. Works Councils. As of 2017, the term of office for works councils was extended from four to five years, and the amount of time off for training was increased to 3 weeks and 3 days. Works Council Elections. To support works council elections, the union offers tools that are designed to facilitate the preparation and execution of works council and youth representative elections. The election assistant guides election committees and company-liaison secretaries through the election process step by step and provides the record to the member registry of the relevant union. The information assistant provides company-liaison secretaries with information about relevant companies and organizations, updates data, and initiates elections. Employee representatives are provided with brochures online and in print, as well as all necessary forms. Brochures for Employee Representatives. From the series "Active in the Works Council": The works council election, rights and obligations, tips for practical work, the representative for employees with disabilities, insurance coverage for employee representatives on supervisory boards and other control bodies. Insurance Coverage. In 2017, insurance coverage for employee representatives on supervisory boards was expanded. All employee representatives (e.g., works council members, personnel representatives, other employee representatives, including group employee representatives) on supervisory boards, advisory boards, and curatorial boards, as well as individuals sent to other control bodies, are now insured, regardless of legal basis and appointment procedures. In cases of violations since January 1, 2017: A maximum of €200,000 per insured person, and a maximum of €2 million per insurance case (if multiple people are involved in an insurance case). No deductible. Youth Representative Conferences. In 2014, a national conference for youth representatives was established. Since then, the conferences have been held annually with approximately 100 participants each time. In workshops, youth representatives have been able to further their education on topics such as health and safety at work, labor law, education, youth protection, democracy, and employee participation. Union Training Programs. The union offers courses such as "Politics, Law, and Economics," "Social Competence," "Supervisory Board Members," or the "Political Union Course." The training programs offer targeted training and development and can be customized for employee representatives.

Trade Union: The curriculum of the Austrian trade unions includes a minimum of 305 instructional units across all federal states. The content, pedagogical, and didactic offerings are divided into three areas of competence: trade union action competence, social competence, and subject-matter competence. To enable participants to deepen their skills in a practical setting, the two-year program includes a European workshop with a study trip to Brussels, a project, self-study phases, and in-depth seminars. In October 2017, the trade union celebrated its 70th anniversary. Furthermore, the trade union's concept was revised, and it will launch a new model in the fall of 2018.
