We present the activity report of the union for the period from July 2022 to June 2024. These two years have been marked by unprecedented challenges, but also by significant victories, at the level of a company, a sector, or for all workers, employed or not. These victories have, more than ever, strengthened and fueled our commitment. We are proud that the union has shown remarkable resilience in the face of the economic and social upheavals that have marked these two years. Thanks to the solidarity and determination of our members and activists, we have been able to make a difference and achieve major progress for workers in Belgium, Europe, and the world.
€ 12
The union mobilized wholeheartedly in the fight for a reform of the law on the minimum wage. We fought for work that is recognized and of high quality, for a fair and equitable tax system, for the protection of everyone's fundamental rights, for democracy, and for the rights of women. Our collective action highlighted the importance of a strong social security system and support towards a just and sustainable transition that creates quality jobs while reducing inequalities.
The union mobilized wholeheartedly in the fight for a reform of the law on the minimum wage, organizing demonstrations and presenting its arguments to Parliament. Our position is clear: we must return to an indicative minimum wage that respects the right to free and fair collective bargaining. The right to demonstrate or strike is fundamental, and any measure that could impact this right must be strictly limited and scrupulously adhere to the principles of legality, proportionality, and necessity.
In 2023, our collective freedoms were clearly threatened. Along with other unions, NGOs, and civil society organizations, we strongly opposed the bill that threatened the fundamental right to demonstrate, highlighting its danger to democracy. It took four mobilizations of tens of thousands of people for this bill to be definitively withdrawn in January 2024. Our mobilization paid off!
The year 2023 undoubtedly marked a turning point in the retail sector in Belgium.

Certain companies have implemented franchise plans that have plunged thousands of workers into complete uncertainty. These workers then embarked on a long and arduous struggle, fueled by anger, determination, and dignity, despite their employers resorting to legal measures to unfairly restrict the exercise of the right to strike, a fundamental right in a democracy. The bankruptcy of the bus manufacturer Van Hool was another significant event during this period. Nearly 1,700 union members were assisted and individually supported in resolving their cases, representing the largest bankruptcy in our country since that of Sabena thirty years ago. Finally, the social year 2024 concluded with the union representatives' campaign during the social elections, which once again demonstrated their commitment and desire to use the voice of workers to make a difference. This is a fundamental moment for democracy within companies: the social elections, which allow workers to elect their representatives to the consultation bodies of their company. The union representatives conducted a vigorous campaign, which once again demonstrated their commitment and desire to use the voice of workers to make a difference. The results confirmed the strength of the union at the sectoral, regional, and national levels of consultation. A decent wage, a healthy and sustainable workplace, less stress, greater job security, more free time, and improved commuting between home and work – these are the priorities that will guide the work of the newly elected union representatives over the next four years. Sharing a common vision, goal, and commitment, and the ability to act in a coordinated and informed manner, are the strengths of the union, a strength that exists every day of the year thanks to the commitment and mobilization of our members and activists in companies, on the streets, during training sessions, or during debates and actions. A sincere and heartfelt thank you to them all! This report reflects our ongoing efforts towards a more equitable and democratic society. It demonstrates our unwavering commitment to our members and our desire to defend the interests of all workers. I wish you a good reading.

Unions and several associations demanded that this provision be removed from the bill because they saw it as a further infringement on the right to collective action.

The union Major advances for temporary workers The rights and obligations of temporary workers can be very different from those of permanent employees. Since the summer of 2022, temporary workers have benefited from several major advances. A victory against the abuse of successive daily contracts By using successive daily contracts, i.e., contracts for a single day, often lasting for extended periods without any guarantee of obtaining a new contract the following day, companies are shifting the cost of ultra-flexible work onto social security. People working under such contracts are more likely to be unemployed, and in case of illness, they may not be eligible for the guaranteed salary paid by the employer. Daily contracts also create a great deal of insecurity for temporary workers, both financially and in terms of organizing their personal lives, and push these workers towards precariousness. In July 2022, after tireless efforts, an agreement was reached at the National Labor Council (CNT) to limit the number of daily contracts in the temporary employment sector. The most important part of this agreement is the introduction of an additional social security contribution to be paid by the employer in cases of abusive use of successive daily contracts. The higher the number of daily contracts (per semester, per temporary worker with the same employer), the higher the social security contribution. This represents a significant advance because it puts a brake on the excessive flexibility imposed by some companies on temporary workers. The agreement, which came into effect on January 1, 2023, is also applicable to student workers, thanks to the pressure exerted by the union. It was also agreed to monitor this new measure, evaluating any unintended consequences aimed at circumventing it (e.g., a shift towards contracts of two or three days, or daily contracts outside of temporary employment). An initial evaluation, completed in May 2024, showed that the new measure has a clear impact on the number of successive daily contracts, as there was a 53% decrease in the number of such contracts in the two semesters of 2023 compared to 2022. It was to be expected that strategies to circumvent the measure would emerge.

Unfortunately, the figures show this. The number of two-day contracts increased by as much as 25% during the same period, and the number of successive daily contracts increased by 11%. The agreement provided for an extension of the measure if this trend were to continue. This new measure generated more than €1 million in additional social security contributions in the first half of 2023. A second evaluation is scheduled to take place within the CNT in the autumn of 2024. A new sectoral agreement. **Endocrine disruptors: it's better to substitute than to cure**

Endocrine disruptors (EDCs) are present in a significant number of household and professional products. However, they are suspected of being the cause of many health problems: they influence our hormones, increase the risk of cancer, obesity, and diabetes. Many workers are exposed to EDCs on a daily basis through the manufacture of products that contain them, or through frequent, prolonged, or high-dose use of certain substances. This is the case, among other things, in the food industry (use of additives), the chemical industry (manufacture of pesticides, cosmetics, fuels, plastics), metallurgy/foundry, the electronics industry, the textile industry, hairdressing, and cleaning. The routes of exposure are multiple: inhalation (fumes, vapors), ingestion (hand to mouth, lack of hygiene), or skin contact (handling substances without personal protective equipment, injuries).

In light of this situation, one of the union's main demands is the development of product labeling and safety data sheets, including information on EDCs. Substituting them with less harmful substances is also a promising avenue for improvement. Often, workers are unaware that they are exposed to such substances. Currently, there is no pictogram that allows for the easy and rapid identification of a substance as being an "EDC."

On March 30, 2023, under pressure from the union, social partners issued a unanimously positive opinion on the inclusion of EDCs in regulations related to well-being. Finally, a national action plan on EDCs was launched in August 2022, with the aim of establishing a comprehensive framework to reduce exposure to EDCs and limit their consequences on the environment and health in Belgium.

In 2023, a pivotal year for retail workers, the year 2023 marked a turning point in the retail sector in Belgium. In January, the management of Intermarché, a French chain that operates exclusively through franchised stores, announced its intention to acquire Mestdagh and legally dismantle the company by converting its stores into company-owned outlets. Excessive judicialization
As observed in many company disputes, social conflict is increasingly being taken to court. The conflict at Delhaize exemplifies how the justice system is being used to excessively limit the exercise of the right to strike, a fundamental right in a democracy.

Increasingly and regularly, employers are filing unilateral requests with the courts. They are obtaining injunctions that effectively nullify any form of collective action, which is essential for building a balance of power. In practice, the employer appears before the court and requests that the judge issue an injunction prohibiting access to a store or warehouse, for example. Because the employer is the only one present, the judge only hears their version of events. The injunction is therefore issued without any adversarial debate, which is a fundamental principle of a fair trial. The employer receives a response within the day. Unions can file an "opposition," but the decision is not rendered for weeks, or even months. In the meantime, the effects of the injunction remain in place, making it practically impossible to organize pickets or rallies outside the stores. In a few months, Delhaize has filed nearly thirty unilateral requests. This is unprecedented in labor history, a blatant display of aggression.

Protecting our members
Legal assistance: provides first-line support, legal assistance, and medical advice related to workplace accidents. Every member of the union is entitled to this.
In the event of a dispute with an employer or with the Onem (National Social Security Office), members can rely on the support of the union's front-line services. These services provide information and advice on labor law and social security, and they support members. If a member is in dispute with their (former) employer, they will contact the relevant professional union to which they belong.

The front-line service will contact the employer to attempt to resolve the dispute amicably (by drafting letters and formal notices, providing a cost estimate for the dispute, and contacting the employer). If the dispute cannot be resolved amicably and requires legal proceedings, the legal department will take over. If a member needs assistance with a case related to a period of unemployment, they should contact the front-line service of their regional union. The union will make the necessary contacts to resolve the issues. It will guide the member through the process, for example, by accompanying them if they are summoned to the unemployment office (Onem) to explain their case. The legal services handle more complex issues related to labor law and social security. They initiate proceedings in the labor court when direct attempts with the employer are ineffective. In the event of bankruptcy, they take steps with the bankruptcy trustee to ensure that the claim is accepted. They then seek compensation from the Business Closure Fund (FFE). If the trustee rejects the claim or the FFE refuses compensation, they initiate proceedings in the labor court. For matters within their jurisdiction, the legal services also defend members against the Onem, the Inami (social security agency), mutual insurance companies, the professional disease fund, and insurance companies. Legal assistance is a union service provided to members. It is fully funded by the solidarity of workers and is an integral part of the union's collective action. Anna and Chris, union representatives: two struggles, one cause.

Anna's (pseudonym) story is not typical. It began in 2014 at a bookstore belonging to the Standaard Boekhandel chain, located in Tielt-Winge, a municipality in the Flemish Brabant region. Five employees of the bookstore were fighting for the payment of a bonus related to the hours they worked on Sundays, to which they were entitled. The employer refused, citing a vague and poorly written agreement. In the spring of 2016, the union forced the employer to conclude a clear and precise collective labor agreement (CLA) on the Sunday work bonus and to agree individually with the employees concerned regarding the payment of bonuses that were due but never paid. Anna, one of the five employees involved, fought this battle with her colleagues and acted as their spokesperson with the employer. In the 2016 social elections, she was elected and became a union representative for Puls. However, the employer viewed Anna's commitment and the fight for the bonus negatively, and shortly after the social elections, announced the closure of the Tielt-Winge bookstore due to lack of profitability. By doing so, the employer avoided the obligation to pay the bonuses for work performed on Sundays, since the CLA no longer applied, its financial burden was reduced due to the change in the joint committee (201 instead of 311), and it decided to dismiss Anna without paying her the protection indemnity to which she was entitled, claiming that the Tielt-Winge bookstore had been closed. The union's fight for the payment of bonuses related to work on Sundays then turned into a long legal battle that lasted for years, during which the union fought alongside Anna. After a positive judgment in the Brussels Labor Court, and then in the Court of Cassation, the union's legal department filed a case with the Antwerp Labor Court, which, on October 9, 2023, confirmed that Standaard Boekhandel was wrong to dismiss Anna based on the closure of a department when there was no definitive cessation of activities, since the privatization of one of the chain's bookstores could not be equated with a company closure.

Anna is therefore entitled to her protection indemnity, which amounts to six years' salary, and Standard Boekhandel is obligated to pay it to her. Commscope is a company that manufactures wireless and fiber optic solutions for the telecommunications industry. It employs approximately 300 people at its Belgian headquarters in Kessel-Lo, near Leuven. On May 6, 2024, the Brussels Labor Court ruled in favor of the union, condemning Commscope for discrimination based on union membership. This decision follows the dismissal of Chris, a union representative for the Metea union, at the end of 2018, without a specific reason and in violation of the law of March 19, 1991, regarding the protection of employee representatives. At the time, this led to a nine-day strike at the company. Following Chris's dismissal, his employer simply paid him severance pay, believing the matter was closed. However, this did not take into account the determination of Chris and the union, who sued the company for discrimination based on union membership. The court confirmed that Chris had been "explicitly targeted because of his role as a representative of the employees and the union." Commscope must therefore pay 20,000 euros in damages due to its discriminatory behavior. This ruling, which sets a precedent, is a strong signal to all employee representatives who represent and defend the rights of their colleagues every day: laws exist to protect them in their commitment, and the union is there to ensure that these laws are respected! This decision is also a serious warning to employers who might be tempted to circumvent the law by dismissing union representatives due to their union involvement. With the help of the union, Sylvie was able to have her illness recognized as work-related and receive the benefits she is entitled to. Sylvie is 42 years old and has worked for 23 years in a poultry processing plant in the Picardy region of Wallonia. She performs repetitive work at a high pace in a refrigerated environment. She is also a union representative at the CPPT, a role that she is passionate about because she develops solutions to reduce musculoskeletal disorders among the company's employees. A few years ago, she began to suffer from carpal tunnel syndrome in both hands. After undergoing surgery on both hands and six weeks of recovery, her right hand began to swell. She was then diagnosed with algodystrophy, a type of inflammation caused by the surgery she had undergone. Doctors told Sylvie that she could no longer work. This was a shock for her. She desperately wanted to return to work because she loves her job and cannot afford to be without an income. However, this illness is not only debilitating at work but also in daily life. Following this diagnosis, Sylvie was unable to work full-time for ten months and then returned to work on a part-time basis. During her period of inability to work, she received a rejection from Fedris, the federal agency for occupational risks, which refused to recognize her illness as a work-related illness, which meant she was not entitled to compensation. She therefore contacted the union for assistance. The union's medical advisor received a very detailed report from the company's occupational health physician about Sylvie's specific work. He also met with her and confirmed the medical analysis and the fact that her illness was indeed work-related. The union initiated legal proceedings in the labor court. On April 29, 2023, the expert appointed by the court recognized Sylvie's periods of inability to work and determined her degree of disability. This allowed Sylvie to finally receive compensation and the benefits she should have received in the past. Today, for financial reasons, Sylvie must return to full-time work. She constantly wears a brace. The refrigerated environment in which she works exacerbates her pain. Whenever possible, her company assigns her to a position where her hand is less stressed. Ideally, Sylvie would like to remain in the company in a different position. If this is not possible, she is considering taking professional training to retrain for a job that does not require her to perform movements that are painful for her now-disabled hand.

Temporary workers are employed by a temporary employment agency and often work for extended periods with the same client company on short-term contracts, week by week or day by day. The "Innovatief" association was created by the union to address the social concerns, union challenges, and questions of Dutch-speaking workers, whether employed or unemployed, their representatives, and the union as a whole. The association offers research-action projects, consultations, and advice. All of Innovatief's activities are focused on people, empowerment, and innovation. Its areas of focus include the labor market, labor relations, the social economy, technological innovation, work organization, sustainable work, employee well-being, education, training, lifelong learning, the environment, climate change, sustainable development, poverty reduction, and union activities.

With its "Doorstart+" project, Innovatief addresses the challenges and provides support in cases of company restructuring, closure, or bankruptcy, for both large and small businesses. It also offers advice to workers looking to reorient their careers. Through personalized support, Innovatief helps workers navigate difficult career transitions and become more resilient in a rapidly changing job market by assisting them in their career changes. Innovatief has also offered inclusion services and advice within companies, in collaboration with advocacy groups, focusing primarily on inclusive work environments and diversity (e.g., combating racism, LGBTQIA+ awareness events). Several seminars were organized, attracting nearly 700 participants. Attention was also paid to the period of Ramadan (with posters, brochures, and advice for union representatives on how to address this period in the workplace). A new project called "Brugfguren" was launched at the end of 2022, building on the "Sherpa" project. Its goal is to help workers navigate the many options available for retraining and professional development.

In 2023, advisors who support and counsel union members in Flanders regarding their careers handled 26,507 email inquiries and requests for information. 84 information sessions were held, with a total of nearly 8,500 participants.

Like sherpas, advisors guide, support, and encourage workers to pursue professional development. As intermediaries between workers, employers, and training organizations, they encourage workers and help them understand the benefits of training. Training sessions are organized for workers who, from their workplace, encourage and, in turn, raise awareness among their colleagues to pursue further training. This Flemish union project is financially supported by the European Social Fund and draws inspiration from similar initiatives in other countries.

**Career Advice:**

In Flanders, a personalized career counseling service is offered to union members at various stages of their careers. This service covers topics such as flexible work arrangements, specialized leave, maternity leave, adoption, foster care, healthcare, career transitions, stress management, telecommuting, and interview preparation. Several webinars and in-person meetings have been organized, with those focused on career transitions being particularly popular and quickly filling up. The counselors also serve as experts during training sessions for union activists. The service also plays a crucial role in providing support during bankruptcies and/or major restructurings. Initially, each worker received their C4 form and then had to apply to the companies that took over the business when they launched recruitment campaigns. A total of 1,642 union members were individually assisted and supported in resolving their issues related to the bankruptcy of their company, which was the largest bankruptcy in the country since that of La Sabena 30 years ago.

Sociopolitical Training for a Better Understanding of Society. The union's training plan is established for four years.
