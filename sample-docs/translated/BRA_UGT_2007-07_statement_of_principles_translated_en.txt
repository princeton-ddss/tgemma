RASIL union union Statement of Principles DeclarationOfPrinciples.indd 1 O) 8/10/2007 22:26:49 DeclarationOfPrinciples.indd 2 8/10/2007 22:27:29 DECLARATION OF PRINCIPLES union - union - CITIZEN SYNDICALISM, INNOVATIVE AND PROGRESSIVE - PREAMBLE We, workers, gathered in São Paulo, capital, on July 19, 20, and 21, 2007, for the founding congress of the union - union - declare our principles, objectives, and commitments. Our common goal is to open a new front for profound changes in society, recognizing the need for a new, offensive historical cycle in the Brazilian labor movement. By conceiving the idea of the union, we are contributing to the construction of new paradigms, looking towards a new labor project that is consistent with the 21st century, centered on the human being, and capable of offering solutions and proposals to national problems. We, at the union, by expressing our opposition to neoliberalism, unemployment, and low wages, condemn slave labor, child labor, and precarious work; we fight against corruption and all forms of prejudice; we do not tolerate hunger and poverty; we will fight against the deterioration of education and healthcare; we advocate for the restoration of Brazil's infrastructure and investment in research and development; we abhor the process of social exclusion, cultural, economic, and political, which is prevalent in the world of work and in most of our population; in short, we believe that our task of defending workers and the Brazilian people is more than just an ethical imperative; it is the basic condition for building and implementing a democratic and transformative project for our country. We, at the union, declare that our objective is to achieve, alongside all democratic and progressive organizations, a model of development that combines: the deepening of political democracy and its extension to the economy, culture, information, and all social spheres; the generalization of citizenship; the democratization and publicization of the state; the progressive recovery of politics and democratic institutions; ethics in politics and public administration; economic growth with income distribution and the creation of decent jobs, with the valorization of the world of work; productive modernization and social justice; the progress of science and technology, and the sovereign and competitive integration of the Brazilian economy into the global economy. We, at the union, declare our solidarity with the struggles of all peoples striving to build a democratic, socially just, peaceful, fraternal, supportive, integrated, multilateral, developed, and environmentally sustainable world. Each principle listed here is not an autonomous and independent sphere from the others; on the contrary, each of them, due to its multidisciplinary and intersectoral nature, constitutes a whole, that is, a complex composed of interconnected and interdependent elements. The Statement of Principles of the union is characterized by an idea of unity in syndicalism, expressing the thinking of its founders, entities, and individuals. Our organization organically combines the magnitude of its objectives with the realism of possibilities; it articulates the present with a view to a better future, and places its trust in the action of women and men, young and old, the forces of democracy and progress, to reveal the vast possibilities of the Nation. Therefore, we affirm that Brazil has the opportunity to recreate itself on democratic, developed, and just foundations. Its future depends on what we, the working men and women, are capable of doing, fighting for, choosing, deciding, and projecting to make the dreams and hopes of millions of citizens a reality. The actions of the organizations affiliated with the union, together with the organized forces of the nation, will offer a new opportunity to rebuild Brazilian society. The sources that illuminate our union work are the Founding Manifesto, the Statement of Principles, and the Social Bylaws of the union; these are three works of syndical engineering, coherently constructed and harmonious with each other, for the benefit of the Brazilian people. They constitute us: 1. Democracy We understand democracy as a universal value and defend its expansion as a permanent strategy to advance democracy in the economic, social, cultural, and informational spheres. 2. EQUALITY We defend concrete equality and reject abstract equality. For the union, equality is achieved through the struggle to change the economic-productive system so that it allows all people to participate in, access, and enjoy the wealth produced and to improve the living conditions of the world of work and the entire population. 3.

SOLIDARITY: We affirm the value of active solidarity, stemming from a profound universal identity of the human species. We aspire to a world that is increasingly integrated, harmonious, and at peace. Therefore, we are guided by the utopia of one day being able to declare the end of privileges and discrimination, and the end of the very concept of "foreigner," which today serves more to fuel hatred than to foster cultural diversity and the plurality of peoples.

4.  CITIZEN SYNDICALISM: We advocate for citizen-based trade unionism, meaning we defend the immediate economic and social interests of workers, but we integrate these interests into the global struggle for the advancement of democracy, freedoms, civil rights, the expansion of citizenship, and the fulfillment of all basic and advanced social needs of the world of work and the Brazilian people. Public policies must reach, include, and serve all workers, who should be seen as integral members of society, not merely as a means of production. This type of trade unionism requires the union and its members to defend the interests of all citizens, even outside of their workplace.

5.  ETHICS: We affirm that ethics presupposes respect for human rights, citizenship, freedoms, environmental protection, and the understanding that public resources cannot be appropriated exclusively or wasted. Similarly, ethics will govern the relationship between the union, its leaders, the leaders and their members, the state, and various institutions of society. Respect for local leaders affiliated to the union is a fundamental condition of ethical trade unionism. This is another requirement that the union will uphold with absolute conviction.

6.  INNOVATION: We advocate for trade union innovation, meaning the exploration of unexplored areas of trade union organization and representation, the relationship between the trade union movement and various socio-political actors, the pursuit of new solutions and rights, the affirmation of universal values that underpin a contemporary trade unionism that addresses the challenges of globalization and an interdependent world. Trade union innovation is the modernization, the creation of something new, the continuous renewal of trade union action in the face of unprecedented problems and new demands in the world of work.

7.  TRADE UNION FREEDOM AND AUTONOMY: We defend trade union freedom and autonomy, and the structure of representation consisting of local unions, federations, confederations, and central labor organizations. Trade union freedom and autonomy, based on internal regulations, will be practiced in relation to national and international economic groups, political parties, churches, and the state.

8.  HUMAN RIGHTS: We reaffirm the universality, indivisibility, interdependence, and interrelatedness of all human rights, including social rights, and particularly labor rights, which are prerequisites for freedom and are therefore inseparable from the equality of citizens, not only before the law, but also in terms of their material and cultural living conditions.

9.  PEACE: We affirm that the forces of peace are stronger than those of war. Therefore, peoples, governments, and all lovers of peace, particularly the global trade union movement, have no more important or urgent task than to preserve life, seek negotiated solutions to local conflicts, and build a solid foundation for universal peace among peoples, a condition essential for the development of all humanity.

10. NEW DEMOCRATIC GLOBAL ORDER: We strive for a democratic global order through the construction of global governance to enable a democratic, just, humane, solidarity-based, environmentally sustainable economic, social, and political order. We will also fight for the reform and democratization of the United Nations and other multilateral institutions.

11. LIBERTARIAN AND HUMANIST VALUES: We draw inspiration from the humanist, libertarian, and solidarity-based heritage of social movements and the struggles of workers in Brazil and around the world.

12. MILLENNIUM DECLARATION: We are committed to achieving the development goals of the Millennium Declaration, namely, the eradication of extreme poverty and hunger; achieving universal primary education; promoting gender equality and empowering women; reducing child mortality; improving maternal health; combating HIV/AIDS, malaria, and other diseases; ensuring environmental sustainability; and developing global partnerships for development, with the aim of achieving a more peaceful, just, and prosperous world.

13. SOCIAL JUSTICE: We affirm social justice as a central value. We are not willing to sacrifice freedom and ethics in its name, as these are also fundamental values of true citizenship that we proclaim.

14. NEOLIBERALISM: We oppose neoliberalism, whose project for Brazil and the world involves a restructuring of the state, the economy, and politics aimed at restricting democracy, eroding workers' rights, removing the state from social policies, and integrating the Brazilian economy into the global economy in a subordinate manner.
15. ORGANIZING THE INFORMAL SECTOR: This innovative union will organize and represent workers in both the formal and informal labor markets, while also working to formalize the informal sector – which currently represents the majority – because we believe that the future of millions of people should not be inevitably defined by a lack of rights.
16. SUSTAINABLE DEVELOPMENT: We advocate for environmental protection and the achievement of sustainable development. Improving the standard of living for people cannot be based on a supposedly moderate form of productivity and consumerism that fails to consider environmental values, the limits of natural resources, and the damage caused by human activity.
17. ENVIRONMENTAL TRADE UNIONISM: We believe that our environmental strategies should not be developed in isolation, but rather integrated with a broader trade union agenda, including collective bargaining, collective agreements, unionization campaigns, human rights, economic policy, workplace health and safety, gender equality, training and education, and should address issues related to the environment and vice versa.
18. ENVIRONMENTAL RESPONSIBILITY: We advocate for the criminal liability of individuals and legal entities that harm the environment, as stipulated in the Federal Constitution. We defend the rational and regulated use of water through the shared management of federal, state, and municipal river basin committees, with the effective participation of workers. As an essential resource for people, the exploitation of water resources should not be influenced by partisan interests, groups, or "lobbies."
19. SUSTAINABLE DEVELOPMENT OF THE AMAZON: We acknowledge our shared responsibility for promoting sustainable development in the Amazon region, and we will seek out scientific, technological, and social innovations to find more appropriate ways to preserve this vast tropical forest as a Brazilian heritage, ensuring the future of the populations that live there. This task cannot be solely the responsibility of Brazil, as misguided policies from neighboring countries can harm the entire ecosystem.
20. SELF-DETERMINATION OF PEOPLES: We defend the right of peoples to self-determination, to live in a democracy under a government of their own free choice, free from all forms of oppression, exploitation, and discrimination. The union, rejecting war as a means of resolving conflicts and opposing all forms of occupation and external intervention, believes that relations between nations should be guided by the Charter of Principles of the United Nations.
21. GLOBALIZATION: We consider globalization a reality full of challenges and opportunities, but it is also a complex and delicate issue that, if not properly managed, can be harmful to the country and its people, and can widen the gap between nations and peoples. We are, therefore, a labor federation committed to overcoming all forms of global imbalances.
22. INTERNATIONALISM: We are committed to the global effort to build and implement new democratic paradigms. We stand in solidarity with all movements for peace, solidarity, and fraternity among peoples, and against all forms of exploitation and oppression. We share and fight for a world that is increasingly integrated, marked by harmonious relations between nations and the free flow of people and ideas.
23. MULTIETHNICITY: We align ourselves with the resolution of the World Conference on Human Rights, which urges states and the international community to promote and protect the rights of people belonging to national or ethnic, religious, and linguistic minorities, in accordance with the Declaration on the Rights of Persons Belonging to National or Ethnic, Religious, and Linguistic Minorities of the United Nations. The union is committed to this global effort and will work to promote the rich multicultural heritage of multiethnicity.
24. UNIVERSAL CITIZENSHIP: We aspire to a universal citizenship, where not only workers' rights, but also political, economic, social, and cultural rights, must be universally guaranteed and exercised.

25. Secularism: Our commitment to the inviolability of freedom of conscience and belief is unwavering. Everyone has the right to profess the religion of their choice or to profess no religion at all. The secular nature of the state is a cornerstone of democracy and civilization, and therefore, an inalienable value. For us, secularism is the space for the freedom of all.

26. Global Labor Action: Dedicated to global labor action, the union will fight for the rights of Brazilian workers working abroad, as well as for the rights of all migrants, regardless of their nationality, who work in the Brazilian labor market.

27. Regional Integration: We affirm that the unity of the peoples of the Americas is an indispensable condition for the development of each nation that comprises it. Integration must be economic, social, political, and cultural, and must involve all institutions, worker participation, and clear social objectives and outcomes. Economic development must be sustainable, preserving natural and historical resources, and maintaining the identity of the peoples of the continent, which encompasses all of the Americas. The union will work towards the construction of a Latin American Community of Nations, in accordance with the Brazilian Constitution.

28. Free Movement of Migration: We will fight to eliminate all barriers to the mobility of workers. We recognize mobility (the ability to come and go) as a universal right of every citizen of the world.

29. Multipolarity and Multilateralism: Aware of the need to protect the credibility of the UN Security Council as a privileged forum for resolving global problems, we do not want a small group of countries to unilaterally decide the fate of nations. What we aspire to is a growing multipolarity, underpinned by the legal and political framework of multilateralism. A world of peace must be both multipolar and multilateral – that is, with the same rules for everyone, even though not all states may be equal.

30. Global Syndicalism and the CSI: We believe that a global economy requires global syndicalism. The union aligns itself with the International Trade Union Confederation (CSI) and its regional affiliates as a means of addressing the challenges of the new economy and of promoting a new form of international labor solidarity.

31. The Union and the CSI: We aspire to actively participate in the affirmation of the CSI as a confederation that represents the entire world of work, and to exercise our autonomy and independence in relation to national and international governments and economic and financial powers.

32. Decent Work: We affirm our objective of achieving decent work in Brazil, which is work that is productive and adequately remunerated, performed under conditions of freedom, equality, and safety, without any form of discrimination, and capable of guaranteeing a dignified life for all people who live off their work, as proclaimed by the ILO.

33. Tripartism: We understand tripartism as the effective space for social dialogue, based on its representation (workers, employers, government). Our participation in forums, councils, or other tripartite management bodies will seek to guarantee the interests of the world of work, always seeking consensus so that deliberations have practical effects for the entire society.

34. Economic Democracy: We understand that democracy cannot end where the economy begins. Democracy and citizenship must be real, full, and participatory, guaranteeing citizens decent work, education, health, housing, security, and professional training. The world of work must participate in the management and decision-making processes of public and private organizations. Profit-sharing and results should be extended to all companies. Budgetary and accounting information from governments and companies should be made available to worker representatives.

35. Organization by Workplace: We believe that for workers, freedom of association remains entirely compromised and limited if it does not exist in practice at the workplace. A union cannot only operate at the doors of companies. We will fight for legislation that guarantees the right to freedom of association at the workplace, with the intervention of the union.


Eradication of Slave Labor and Child Labor: The union will relentlessly fight for the eradication of slave labor, or analogous forms of exploitation, and the exploitation of child labor. These practices are inhumane, operate in secrecy, and are marked by authoritarianism, corruption, social segregation, racism, clientelism, and a disregard for human rights. We advocate that such practices be classified as unbailable crimes, and in cases where they occur in rural areas, the land should be transferred to agrarian reform programs.

37. Eradication of Discrimination in the Workplace: We strive for all workers to have guaranteed equal opportunities and treatment, regardless of ethnic origin, gender, religion, sexual orientation, political opinion, nationality, social origin, and economic situation, as well as other characteristics that may define them.

38. Combating Moral Harassment in the Workplace: We will combat moral harassment in the workplace and encourage affiliated organizations to include specific clauses in collective agreements to address and prevent moral harassment. Moral harassment in the workplace includes, but is not limited to, exposing workers to humiliating or distressing situations, or any action, word, or gesture repeatedly and continuously performed during working hours by a supervisor or superior who, in the exercise of their functions, abuses the authority granted to them, with the aim or effect of damaging the subordinate's self-esteem and autonomy, thereby harming the work environment, the services provided, and hindering the subordinate's career advancement or job security.

39. Pluralism of Ideas: We affirm that the union, in its organizational policy, will express its democratic and pluralistic vision, both in the functioning of its internal structures and in its relationship with other trade unions and social and political movements, overcoming the culture of discrediting both opponents and allies. Pluralism of ideas is a cornerstone of civilization and must not be compromised.

40. Participatory Management: We reaffirm our unwavering commitment to the democratic decision-making process through participatory management, ensuring continuous dialogue with members, freedom of expression, tolerance for diversity, transparency in actions, and agility in internal and external communication. The union will promote unity in diversity around the common interests of the working world.

41. Unity in Diversity: We advocate for unity in diversity. Divergent opinions, both within and outside the union, will be respected, while the union will work to ensure unity in the struggle for the common interests of the working world.

42. Trade Union Unity: We consider trade union unity and trade union democracy to be strategic objectives and essential factors for strengthening the bargaining power of unions, federations, confederations, and trade union centers in promoting rights and defending the autonomy of Brazilian trade unionism.

43. Education in Human Rights: We emphasize the importance of including the topic of Human Rights in Brazilian education programs at all levels. We will fight for education to promote understanding, tolerance, peace, and friendly relations between nations and all groups of individuals, without any distinction of sex, nationality, ethnicity, sexual orientation, or religion, and we will support the development of activities by those who strive to achieve these goals.

44. Political Reform: We understand political reform as the expansion of political democracy and freedoms, the strengthening of political parties, the democratization of public administration, the strengthening of the rule of law and its institutions, the democratization of power, the creation of a democratic political culture, and administrative decentralization. We advocate for transparency in the actions of public officials, citizen control and veto power over representatives, and therefore, we affirm that the current political reform in Brazil should not be limited to electoral rules.

45. Local Power: We believe that new sets of decisions should be transferred to local representative bodies, where citizens live, participate, make decisions, and exercise oversight. Deepening democracy necessarily involves shifting power and responsibilities from the federal and state levels to various local levels.

46.

Legislative Policy: We will maintain a proper and necessary relationship with the National Congress, Legislative Assemblies, and Municipal Councils in order to develop laws that benefit the world of work, society as a whole, and sustainable development.

47. Tax Reform: We advocate for the simplification of the tax system, the reduction of taxes on production and basic goods, and for the tax burden to fall primarily on consumption. We advocate for the creation of a progressive tax system that is widely computerized in real time, taxing those who can afford it more, including on consumption, without harming production, but strongly impacting the profits of banks and financial speculation.

48. Social Security Reform: We advocate for a public, universal, and basic social security system that guarantees acquired rights and expected rights, ensures the maintenance of the real value of benefits, repeals the Social Security Factor, prevents the increase of the minimum retirement age, allows for its multipartite social control (government, employers, workers, retirees, and pensioners), combats tax evasion and avoidance, and makes it universal through the unification of the formal and informal sectors, with the clear objective of guaranteeing a social security system for the future that ensures the dignity and citizenship of present and future generations.

49. Agrarian Reform: We are in favor of agrarian reform with a comprehensive registry of families in need. We believe that simply owning land does not solve the problem. There must be extensive technical and credit assistance. Properties should be transferred on a temporary basis, without the possibility of sale. The union will discuss and defend agrarian reform at all levels of government and organized civil society.

50. Urban Reform: We advocate for the need for urban reform, as the right to housing is legally recognized as a fundamental right by international human rights treaties of which the Brazilian state is a signatory and legally obligated, based on Article 6 of the Brazilian Constitution. It should include legal security of tenure, physical habitability conditions, affordability, accessibility, cultural appropriateness, access to basic infrastructure and services, and good location. Urban properties whose owners use slave labor should be expropriated for the purposes of urban reform.

51. Agricultural Policy and Rural Workers: We advocate for an agricultural policy that supports rural workers—whose rights continue to be violated—and small farmers, who continue to be excluded from the world of large agribusiness, in order to ensure, for the former, improved living conditions, working conditions, wages, and decent employment relationships, and for the latter, access to differentiated credit, technology, and markets.

52. Judicial Reform: We advocate that the primary goal of judicial reform is to end impunity and to provide broad, rapid, and affordable access to justice for our people, contrary to the current trend of centralization, delay, and concessions to the corporatism of judges, lawyers, and notaries, who do not allow for social and democratic control of the judicial power. At the same time, the union's relationship with the judicial power will take into account the priority of social interests, to the detriment of interests that are contrary to the world of work and society.

53. Union Reform: We advocate for a union system composed of organization in the workplace, unions, federations, confederations, and a central labor organization, organized freely by the world of work, according to the Federal Constitution.

54. Trade, Service, and Industrial Policy: We advocate for the adoption in Brazil of a policy of ethical, fair, and solidarity trade, as well as a policy of service and industry built between the government, workers, and companies. The challenges of the globalized world require the creation of an environment conducive to the development of new sectors and the support and modernization of those in which we have all the links in the production chain. This challenge will not be overcome by the blind forces of the market.

55. Indigenous Peoples: We recognize the inherent value of indigenous peoples as cultures and as social and political organizations within the Brazilian nation. We call on all Brazilians to value the permanence of indigenous peoples and to promote the conditions for indigenous people to have their lands guaranteed and to use them for their well-being and the preservation of their cultures and the environment.

56. Combating Corruption: We will combat corruption, which affects the public sector and various segments of society. We call on all those who wish to join the fight against this, which is one of the great ills of our nation.

The union advocates for the creation of social control mechanisms that allow for greater oversight and transparency of public resources.

57. Regarding the Employer
We believe that the position of the trade union movement should not be to propose or accept the exchange of economic and social gains for workers for false promises of creating new jobs or maintaining existing ones, promises that are often not fulfilled. The union's position is to uncompromisingly defend the improvement of wages and the gains of the working world, while also advocating for an economic policy that allows the country to grow, generate millions of jobs, and distribute and increase income.

58. Quality of Public Service
We advocate for a quality public service and the dignity of public servants, as well as mutual respect between public servants and citizens. The establishment of effective public policies and a quality public service are linked to the professionalization of public administration, with a drastic reduction in appointed positions, competitive exams as the sole means of entry, the stability, and the qualification of public servants in state functions. We support ILO Convention 151 and Resolution 159. The regulation of collective bargaining and the establishment of a base date are necessary conditions for regulating the right to strike, which cannot be prevented from being used as a legitimate instrument.

59. Reduction of Working Hours
We believe in the principle that everyone should work fewer hours so that everyone can work. Therefore, the union will fight for a reduction in working hours in Brazil, especially considering that the recent increases in productivity can be absorbed by companies without loss of competitiveness. We advocate for respect for collective agreements that have already achieved a reduction in working hours, and any changes to their clauses should only be in the direction of reducing the negotiated working hours.

60. Income Distribution
We advocate that the most effective policy for income redistribution in Brazil is the sustainable increase in real wages, made possible by both productivity gains and the defense of workers' rights, the inclusion of millions in production and services, and the mutual enhancement of resources allocated to social public policies, which need to grow in proportion to the GDP and have their allocation socially controlled.

61. Constitutional Minimum Wage
We advocate for the preservation of the value of the minimum wage as a floor for social security benefits and the creation of a commission composed of trade union confederations, the National Congress, and the Federal Executive Branch to establish a policy for the appreciation of the national minimum wage in the coming years, in order to fully comply with the Federal Constitution, which establishes that the minimum wage is national and must be sufficient to support a worker and their family, covering expenses not only for food, but also for housing, education, health, leisure, clothing, transportation, hygiene, and social security.

62. FGTS (Guarantee Fund for Time of Service)
We advocate for an increase in the profitability of the FGTS, which currently corresponds to the Reference Rate (TR) plus interest of 3.0% per year. Its remuneration can and should be improved because the combination of its investment period (longer than the market) and its interest rate (lower than the market) deprives participants in the fund of a considerable gain that could be obtained with safety in other market investments.

63. Specialized Training
We understand that with the introduction of new technologies, new forms of work and production organization are emerging. The union encourages its members to negotiate with companies and employer associations the inclusion of clauses that develop actions and programs for the education and training of employed workers, to enable them to use the innovations introduced.

64. CPMF (Financial Transaction Tax)
We advocate that the CPMF should be exempt from salary accounts and their subsequent transactions. In return, its collection should be exclusively for supervisory purposes, and the proceeds from its collection should be used to finance public health.

65. Quality Public Education
We advocate for education, knowledge, information, and communication as the central elements of material progress, intellectual development, and human well-being. We advocate for an educational policy based on the generalization of public schools, the reorientation of the content of education at all levels to transform it into an analytical, empowering, and innovative education, the training or retraining of teachers, and the construction of a quality public education system that is in tune with modernity.

66.

**Health and the SUS (National Health System)**

We consider health to be equivalent to the quality of life and, therefore, it encompasses issues such as wages, housing, sanitation, education, food, leisure, a healthy environment, and culture. We advocate for a radical program of humanization of healthcare services that values all healthcare professionals, from community health workers to doctors, and that staunchly defends the SUS and its improvement, including its management, in order to make it a public health system that truly fulfills the constitutional mandate that health is a right of all citizens and a duty of the state.

**67. Labor Relations, Health, and Safety**

We understand that one of the main tools for protecting workers in a company is the CIPA (Internal Commission for Accident Prevention), which aims to prevent accidents and illnesses arising from work, in order to ensure that work is always compatible with the preservation of life and the promotion of the health of workers. The union encourages its affiliated organizations to strengthen the CIPA in all its workplaces and to demand the implementation of the Regulatory Norms (both urban and rural).

**68. Food and Nutritional Security**

We defend the right of all to a healthy, accessible, and high-quality diet, in sufficient quantities and on a permanent basis. This is what we call food and nutritional security. It must be based on dietary practices that promote health, without ever compromising access to other essential needs. This is a right of citizenship, the right to be properly nourished, respecting the specific cultural characteristics of each region. And Brazil, like all sovereign countries, has the duty to guarantee food and nutritional security for its people, and the union will fight to make this right a reality.

**69. Science and Technology**

Aware that the benefits of scientific and technological advancements are currently distributed unequally among countries and within each country, we will fight to eliminate this "scientific-technological apartheid" in order to create real opportunities and ensure that everyone can benefit from them. In short, the achievements of science and technology must be democratized for the benefit of society and the humanization of work.

**70. Qualification and Retraining**

We advocate for a comprehensive program of qualification, retraining, and education to train workers, young people, and all segments of the population at risk, in order to improve their level of knowledge and technical-professional skills to better face the labor market. This program should be funded by resources from the Worker Support Fund (FAT) and developed in partnership with trade unions and social organizations. The FAT should not be used for any purpose other than those for which it was created.

**71. Sexual Harassment and Exploitation**

We will combat violence based on gender and all forms of sexual harassment and exploitation, including those resulting from cultural prejudices, homophobia, and international trafficking, as they are incompatible with the dignity and value of the individual and must be eliminated. This can be achieved through legal measures and national and international cooperation in areas such as socio-economic development, education, maternity, healthcare, and social assistance.

**72. Public Safety**

We advocate for public safety as an essential right: a condition for life, freedom, and citizenship. An effective public safety policy requires replacing reactive improvisation and sporadic voluntarism with planning and structural investments. It requires training, professional development, and recognition; knowledge management; the creation of a common and accessible database for all security institutions; managerial reform; intelligent prospecting; efficiency; reactive monitoring; analysis of criminal patterns; technology and scientific investigation; an end to impunity; reform of the Penal Code; and external control through independent ombudsman offices and corporate-wide supervisory bodies as important tools to combat corruption and ensure respect for human rights, as well as the creation of a national public safety network, in the form of a Unified Public Safety System.

**73. Policy for the Empowerment of Women**

We advocate for the creation of a society that allows for the expression and expansion of female subjectivity and that overcomes the sexual division of labor and the current challenges faced by women within the social, political, and economic structures, which are still based on a relationship of power between the sexes, and which continue to disadvantage women.

The union, as stipulated in its bylaws, will ensure that at least 30% of its governing bodies are composed of women.

74. YOUTH POLICY
We advocate for the creation of a society where young people have real opportunities and possibilities for full development, according to their aspirations. Social marginalization is not their sole and inevitable condition. Therefore, more than vocational schools, training, and qualification (which are essential for their proper integration into the labor market and, consequently, for ensuring their citizenship), young people need an education that allows them to participate in the Information Age and become full citizens.

75. POLICY FOR SENIORS
We advocate for a society where seniors have all the rights of citizenship guaranteed, without discrimination of any kind, with their participation in the community ensured, their dignity and well-being preserved, and the right to a decent social security system. We fight for the creation of conditions that make it possible for them to live in society, feeling useful and happy.

76. CULTURE
We advocate for a cultural policy that creates opportunities for the development of various popular and working-class cultural expressions, that promotes both industrial and independent production, that values the regional diversity of Brazilian culture, that creates bridges for dialogue and cooperation between popular and academic cultural producers, that strengthens the artistic and cultural production of a popular nature and decentralizes it in the pursuit of valuing cultural workers and democratizing access to the country's cultural assets. The union will seek to unite cultural movements, in all their diversity, in their difficult struggle to counter monopolies, and to establish itself as an essential component in the construction of a new citizenship, closely linked to the world of work and its values.

Your affiliated organizations will value their Cultural Departments, Secretariats, and Directorates, encouraging the creation of cultural spaces in union headquarters and facilities to promote activities, performances, and shows aimed at raising the cultural level of the working class.

77. INTELLECTUALS
We advocate for a close link between the world of work and intellectuals, producers of culture, science, and knowledge. Furthermore, given the growing importance of technical expertise in production, services, and the struggles of the world of work, the union believes that, in the face of the scientific and technological revolution, intellectuals are a crucial ally, as they also have democratic aspirations, promote the defense and expansion of culture, advocate for freedom of creation, and support democratic control of the cultural industry, as well as the elevation of the cultural level of the world of work and the Brazilian people.

78. SPORTS AND LEISURE
We will reject the old notion that sports and leisure are merely activities related to free time (for the unemployed and the recreational activities of the financially privileged) and that they are unimportant from the perspective of the old labor movement. The union understands sports and leisure as an inalienable right of the world of work, given what they mean in terms of physical, biopsychic, aesthetic, and social well-being of the body, as well as a socio-cultural phenomenon that shapes citizenship. Recognizing that experiencing sports and leisure requires awareness of the right to access cultural assets, the union will organize national and permanent initiatives and will guide its affiliated organizations to fight for the inclusion of clauses in agreements and conventions that promote sports and leisure for workers and their families.

79. DEMOCRATIC SOCIETY OF INFORMATION
We believe that there cannot be a democratic society of information without paying particular attention to the special needs of marginalized and vulnerable groups in society, such as the unemployed, less privileged individuals, seniors, and people with disabilities, for whom digital inclusion technology must be accessible. We will combat illegal acts, as well as those motivated by racial discrimination, xenophobia, and related intolerances, hatred, violence, all forms of abuse against children, including pedophilia and child pornography, and human trafficking and exploitation.

80. MICRO AND SMALL BUSINESSES
We will develop actions to support micro and small businesses throughout the country, recognizing their role in creating jobs and generating income.

81. SOCIAL RESPONSIBILITY
We advocate for the adoption of good practices in corporate social responsibility.

The union encourages all its affiliated entities to include discussions about the implementation of corporate social responsibility policies in their agendas. These policies should be based on Brazilian and international standards related to the subject. Currently, many companies engage in philanthropy or social assistance by making unilateral decisions, "embellishing" their social reports, which is completely contrary to the best practices of a genuine corporate social responsibility policy.

82. The Third Sector
We understand that the labor movement, in its role of defending the world of work and its aspirations for human emancipation, has in the Third Sector a fundamental ally. It should establish partnerships with this sector in areas that have interfaces with the union's priorities. Therefore, the union, dedicated to an action that goes beyond the company, will develop joint initiatives with the diverse entities that operate within the Third Sector, promoting the world of work, culture, science, ecology, and life in all its vastness.
