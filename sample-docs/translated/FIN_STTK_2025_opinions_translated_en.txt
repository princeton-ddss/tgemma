A skilled workforce. High-quality and accessible education forms the foundation of societal progress. Skills. The trade union aims to strengthen everyone's right to lifelong learning and professional development. Good skills and the opportunity for continuous learning throughout one's career promote equal opportunities for participation in working life. Investing in skills strengthens employment, prevents social exclusion, and improves equality and fairness in society. Investments in skills and employee well-being create the conditions for improving productivity. The green transition and technological development are rapidly changing the skills needs and professional structures of the working world. The importance of skills is particularly highlighted in ensuring a just transition. The actual opportunities for employees to improve their education and skills or to train for a new profession must be improved. There is a shortage of skilled workers in almost all sectors and regions. In addition to highly educated specialists, there is a shortage of skilled workers in various fields. In addition to strengthening the skills of Finns, it is necessary to increase labor and education-related immigration and to fully utilize the skills of international workers. Workplace as a central learning environment. A workplace can function well and productively when everyone's skills are recognized and utilized, and when it is possible to develop skills. Workplaces need measures to promote a learning-oriented culture and strengthen collaborative learning. New and better products and working methods emerge in workplaces through the development of skills. Opportunities for skills development are distributed very unevenly in workplaces. Employers' investments in the development of employees' skills have been declining for some time. The systematic development and management of skills in workplaces must be strengthened by implementing skills assessments. In addition, the employer must draw up a comprehensive training plan for the entire workplace each year. The opportunities for individuals to improve their skills and education levels throughout their careers must be strengthened. Changing skills needs. The EU's goal is that 60 percent of the working-age population will participate in continuous learning each year. Achieving this goal requires determined action to ensure a relevant and high-quality supply of continuous learning and guidance services. Participation in continuous learning should be possible for all working-age people, regardless of their financial situation. The barriers to accessing education must be lowered. Education must be easily accessible and affordable. Education leading to a qualification must be free of charge. Study benefits must ensure sufficient income during studies in different life situations. A new form of support is needed to replace the abolished adult education allowance, which ensures income during a period of study leave. Development of the education system. The long-term development and resourcing of the education system create the foundation for skills and learning. Long-term investments must be made in the well-being of children and young people, early childhood education, basic education, and secondary and higher education. Skilled teachers and other staff, sufficient resources, good working conditions, and management are important enablers of high-quality education. In the future, education must be able to better meet the individual support and guidance needs of students. Gender-based choices are clearly visible when young people transition from basic education to vocational and higher education. A diverse and high-quality understanding of working life is needed to mitigate gender-based educational choices. Students' and young people's mental health problems have increased. Education must strengthen a sense of community, participatory practices, and learner-centeredness. Sustainable economy. The trade union believes that the economy should support a just, sustainable, and prosperous Finland. Strengthening employment and skills, the sustainability of the tax and pension system, and combating climate change are key to this. Supporting economic growth. Finland's aging population and the economic downturn caused by the coronavirus crisis require Finland to take measures to support economic growth. Economic recovery measures and investments should be directed to support new growth areas, climate policy objectives, and skills development. Adequate funding for research and innovation is needed for projects that utilize new technologies and digitalization to create new jobs. This can improve Finland's competitiveness and its ability to respond to structural changes. Sustainable taxation. The focus of taxation should be shifted from labor to property and environmental taxes. In particular, the purchasing power of low- and middle-income earners must be protected through taxation. At the same time, the taxation of capital income and assets must be developed to support the tax base. Taxation should also encourage the promotion of climate action.

High Employment
The Finnish welfare model is built on a high employment rate. In recent years, the employment rate has increased, but we still need measures to further increase it in the long term. High employment requires active investment in labor market policy and effective, well-targeted, and high-quality employment services. To speed up the employment of those who are difficult to employ, such as the elderly, young people, immigrants, and those with disabilities, it is necessary to strengthen personalized services. The resources for labor market services in Finland must be brought up to the Nordic level. Support for reduced working hours would facilitate the return to work on a part-time basis for employees who have been laid off, dismissed, or otherwise lost their jobs. Temporary elimination of employer contributions or wage subsidies should be considered, for example, when hiring immigrants or unemployed people over the age of 55. In a rapidly changing world and economy, we need better safety nets to speed up the re-employment of those who have been laid off. This is especially important for improving training opportunities for those who have been laid off. International recruitment should be seen as a way to increase the employment rate. The processing of work-based residence permit applications should be streamlined, and we need national recruitment initiatives for sectors with labor shortages.

Green Transition
Climate change has been a central theme of international cooperation and policy for a long time, and its importance will continue to grow in the future. The political decisions made to slow down climate change, together with technological development, have led to a green transition in economies around the world. The transition from fossil fuels to emission-free energy in energy production, transportation, and industry is leading to many changes in the economy and working life. At the same time, this is an important issue for the Finnish trade union movement.

Moving Away from Fossil Fuels
The green transition is leading to changes in the economy and working life, and it creates new opportunities for investment and new jobs. For Finland, the green transition offers the opportunity to replace a large part of fossil fuel imports with exports of new products and services. This requires bold political decisions from Finland, as well as investments in skills. Finland is at the forefront of the world in the production of clean and affordable energy. This gives us a competitive advantage on which to build an investment-friendly environment. Moving away from fossil fuels also supports Finland's self-sufficiency and reduces the impact of geopolitical crises.

Just Transition
For employees, fairness is important in the green transition. A just transition ensures that:
* Employees have the opportunity to update their skills for a new profession if necessary.
* The costs of the transition are not excessively high for any group of people.
* The benefits are distributed as fairly as possible.

Finland has the potential to succeed in the green transition, but international competition is fierce and the pace of change is rapid. In addition to affordable and clean energy, it is crucial how well we succeed in increasing the production of green technology products and services based on skills. By investing in skills, we can keep employees involved in the changes as much as possible and support the implementation of an economically, socially, and regionally just transition. This will also ensure the broad political support needed for the green transition among citizens.
