The 15th union conference, held from February 12-14, 2016, reaffirmed the decisions made by previous conferences and adopted the following resolution, based on the principles of the union, to promote the rights of the working class and establish a free and democratic society:

The union convened under conditions created by global capitalism that undermine the fundamental rights and freedoms of the working class. The global crisis that began in 2008 has once again demonstrated that crises are an inherent feature of capitalism. The neoliberal ideology, which has been promoted for the past 30 years by capitalist circles as a panacea, has proven to be a failure. Globalization has brought only poverty, unemployment, war, and destruction to the world's people. The network of national and international relations that has emerged in the interests of transnational capital is no different from the "wild capitalism" that was used to describe the early stages of capitalism. In the pursuit of "maximum profit," global capital has commodified everything, leaving nothing untouched. A world has been created where even the labor of women and children is exploited mercilessly, and migrant workers are subjected to inhumane conditions. This process has had a profound impact on labor-capital relations and on production relations as a whole. Transnational capital has developed its competitiveness through the exploitation of unorganized and cheap labor, and a "market economy without rules" has been attempted to be imposed worldwide. These developments have inevitably led to problems such as lack of organization, union-busting, privatization, subcontracting, job insecurity, and wage stagnation, which have become major obstacles to the labor movement. Job insecurity has become the most widespread form of employment in the world, with 1.8 billion people worldwide forced to work in the informal sector. In our country, migrant workers are also subjected to the most brutal forms of informal employment. The welfare state has been undermined, both in terms of its understanding and its institutional functioning. This has not only eroded the individual rights of workers but has also weakened the social position of the working class. Globalization was promised to be a paradise, but the reality for workers and the working class around the world has been more unemployment, lower incomes, more hunger, poverty, and social fragmentation. The world is entering a new century characterized by widening income disparities between countries and people, deteriorating living conditions for workers, the spread of precarious and cheap labor, and persistent unemployment. Lack of organization is becoming an inevitable part of these new conditions, and the unionization rate of the working class has fallen to an average of 6%. Over the past 30 years, the world has moved further away from peace, and a state of near-constant war has emerged on the planet. Globalization has created nothing but a future of death for humanity, along with the economic and social distortions it has caused. In recent years, our region has become one of the "hottest" regions in the world. The most brutal consequences of globalization are being exposed in our region. The deaths of millions of people and the displacement of tens of millions are clear evidence of the magnitude of the tragedy. These developments have also reached a level that threatens our most basic rights and freedoms in Turkey. Turkey entered the 21st century struggling with crises and under the oppressive conditions created by the September 12 military junta. The neoliberal market practices that began in Turkey with the decisions of January 24, 1980, aimed to achieve economic growth and development through the exploitation of cheap labor. In order to ensure the continuity of this system, a military coup was carried out on September 12, 1980. The new laws prepared under the conditions of September 12 hindered union organization, and the collective bargaining system was designed to cover only a very small minority of workers. On the one hand, the continuation of informal employment, and on the other hand, the spread of subcontracting and flexible work arrangements, achieved the goals foreseen by the decisions of January 24, 1980, while also creating major obstacles to the organization of the labor movement and the working class. After the year 2000, the same trend continued, and job insecurity, which had spread in the market, was enshrined in legislation, and restrictions and prohibitions that suppressed the labor movement continued.

The new Labor Law of 2003 (Law No. 4857) and the new Trade Unions and Collective Bargaining Agreements Law of 2012 (Law No. 6356) clearly demonstrate that the AKP government, through its January 24th decisions and as a continuation of the September 12th regime, continues to exist with pro-capital policies. All discussions regarding the new employment structure, particularly severance pay and "flexible security," are nothing more than attempts to create a new environment conducive to these goals and to overcome existing obstacles. The intended objectives and applications aim to achieve economic development and reduce unemployment by weakening workers' protections and eliminating their acquired rights. The rigid employment structure that is claimed as a justification for the desired changes can only be argued from a pro-capital perspective and a market-oriented, neoliberal ideology. The reality that is being obscured by this ideological attack is that Turkey ranks among the countries with the lowest wages, the longest working hours, the fewest vacation days, the highest mortality rates at work, the lowest unionization rates, and the least job security. Regarding the conditions faced by all workers in Turkey, any talk of equality is only possible if we acknowledge the inherent inequality stemming from the primitive working conditions that lead to the exploitation of workers. The Turkish economy is structured according to the interests of international capital. In this sense, Turkey is one of the countries where neoliberal policies are implemented most decisively. However, it is precisely these policies that create social problems. Despite all claims of economic development, Turkey lags far behind global averages in terms of social development indicators. For example, Turkey, the 17th largest economy in the world, ranks first in worker deaths, 126th in women's rights, 61st in healthcare, 99th in political democracy, and 109th in education. Furthermore, according to OECD data, Turkey is one of the three countries with the most unequal income distribution, along with Chile and Mexico. It is also among the countries with the lowest unionization rates in the world. More than 95% of workers lack collective bargaining protections. Unemployment has been steadily increasing in recent years and has taken on a structural nature. The AKP government has been attempting to restructure labor relations and working conditions for workers by eliminating their acquired rights. The National Employment Strategy Document, included in annual plans and government programs from 2012 to 2016, is an attack document aimed at achieving economic development by eliminating workers' most basic rights. The employment policies implemented by the AKP include measures that will eliminate severance pay, create widespread precarious employment, eliminate job security, and turn workers into temporary employees through private employment agencies. Today, Turkey has institutionalized the most rigid and worker-unfriendly economic and social structure in the world, by controlling and suppressing unions and the labor movement, making trade union freedoms and the right to collective bargaining unusable, and making strike bans and postponements a regular occurrence, leading to an extremely narrow scope of collective bargaining. Turkey, integrated into global capitalism in line with the interests of international capital, is resisting democratic change in order to maintain the development achieved through a low-wage labor model. At the same time, this accumulation regime is causing enormous problems in the areas of worker health and safety in our country, leading to an increase in workplace accidents. This resistance is manifesting itself in various forms in response to the evolving situation, and is gradually escalating towards a level where all democratic rights are suspended. The AKP government cannot achieve its economic, social, and political goals without a strong labor movement and opposition. Therefore, it is increasingly forced to become a more repressive regime. The recent repressive practices, arrests, and examples of extrajudicial trials clearly demonstrate this. In Turkey today, trade union rights and freedoms can only be exercised within the framework envisioned by the September 12th junta. There is no democratic change in areas such as the right to strike, union membership, and the right to collective bargaining. The social state and social rights have been largely eliminated. The policies implemented by the AKP government to solve social problems reflect a "one-party rule" mentality. The government shapes and directs the state's coercive apparatus and the justice system entirely according to its own interests. It constantly sends the message that no one, except itself, can feel safe, and that no institution or organization can be trusted.

In the current environment, those who dissent face severe consequences, even the slightest objection is met with violence, and any form of different thinking is used as grounds for accusations. The pursuit of peaceful and conciliatory solutions to the Kurdish issue has been completely blocked by the government. The social problem is treated solely as a "security issue," creating an environment that threatens the future and peace of the entire society. Media outlets, opposition groups, intellectuals, and academics are being subjected to intimidation. Under these conditions, a new constitutional debate is also taking place in Turkey. Turkey is governed by a repressive constitution from 1980, which brought the fascist military regime and suppressed labor movements and democratic forces. The demand to amend the 1982 constitution, a product of a highly repressive and authoritarian era, has been voiced since its enactment. Today, the conditions in our country make the need for a liberal, egalitarian, democratic, and social constitution an unavoidable imperative. However, constitutions are documents of social consensus. They outline the fundamental principles and framework for a society to live together. In the current environment of tension, polarization, and conflict, a liberal and democratic new constitution cannot be created. The nature of any constitutional amendment carried out under these conditions can be clearly seen by examining the changes made in 2010. Recent developments and proposals put forward by the ruling AKP party and the President point towards an anti-democratic, authoritarian regime, framed within a presidential system. All of this evidence suggests that the constitutional debate initiated by the AKP government does not aim to democratize Turkey. Based on these findings, our 15th union has decided to pursue the following goals in the coming period:

1.  **A DEMOCRATIC, LIBERAL, AND EQUAL CONSTITUTION:** The primary task is to raise the demand for a democratic, liberal, and egalitarian constitution and spread it throughout society, creating an environment conducive to democratic constitutional change. We will continue our struggle with all our might against any form of authoritarian, repressive, divisive, and freedom-restricting governance imposed by the government, against fascism.

2.  **A DEMOCRATIC AND EQUITABLE SOCIETY:** To overcome the increasingly polarized and conflict-ridden mindset, and to defend democracy and peace in all areas with the motto of brotherhood among workers, is one of our primary tasks. Democracy and peace are universal and indispensable goals for the working class, and our Confederation is committed to fighting for these goals. We will fight for a peaceful resolution to the Kurdish issue based on equality, freedom, and fraternity, while opposing violence and oppression.

3.  **FAIR AND EQUITABLE DISTRIBUTION OF INCOME:** Our country must be removed from the list of countries with the most unequal income distribution, and a fair and balanced income distribution must be achieved. To this end, increasing the minimum wage, promoting union organization, and expanding the collective bargaining system are our primary goals.

4.  **DIGNIFIED AND SECURE EMPLOYMENT:** We will continue to fight in all areas to improve the working conditions of the working class and to ensure dignified and secure employment. We will create the broadest possible front of struggle to prevent informal employment, precarious work, subcontracting, and all forms of employment that lead to exploitation.

5.  **MIGRANT WORKERS:** We will work to ensure that Syrian migrant workers, who have become part of the Turkish working class, and all other migrant workers are freed from inhumane working conditions, and that they enjoy all rights, including access to public services, equal working conditions, and the right to organize, based on the principle of class solidarity. In this regard, we will resolutely fight against racism, discrimination, and anti-immigrant sentiment.

6.  **WORKER HEALTH AND SAFETY:** We will continue our efforts to ban precarious work relationships, especially subcontracting, to ensure the security of all workers, to remove obstacles to trade union freedom, and to establish an autonomous and democratic institutional structure involving unions, universities, and professional organizations/associations, in order to implement a new system.

7.  **SICK PAY IS A CORNERSTONE OF JOB SECURITY:** The right to severance pay, which has been achieved through a long struggle and is an important pillar of job security, will be protected. We will not allow any rollback of our existing rights through any regulations related to severance pay, under any circumstances, using all democratic and legitimate means.
