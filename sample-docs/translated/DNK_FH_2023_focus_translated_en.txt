1.  **A Binding Commitment to Organization**
The rate of unionization in the Danish labor market has been declining for over 25 years. Today, only 50% of the workforce is a member of a trade union or affiliated organization, and in the private sector, it is now more common to be unorganized than organized. The member organizations of the trade union are responsible for organizing and retaining more members, and this is an ongoing effort. However, there is a common challenge – across professions, education levels, industries, and age groups – that calls for a joint, cross-cutting effort that, where relevant, can supplement the work of member organizations in reversing membership trends. Therefore, a shared goal is to reverse the decline in membership for the trade union during the upcoming congress period. In close collaboration with member organizations, the trade union will work to develop and implement a series of initiatives that will contribute to the effort of organizing and retaining more members within the trade union's member organizations. A forum will be created with a focus on organization and retention, where knowledge and experiences are shared, and progress is monitored. The goal is also to follow up on existing analyses and develop new ones, and to launch new initiatives, including ensuring that fewer members fall through the cracks and with a particular focus on initiatives targeted at young people. These initiatives may be implemented for all member organizations or specifically for certain groups. The principle is that every new or retained member is in the interest of the collective. The trade union will also support the creation of a connection between the central initiatives and the local work of the trade union, and encourage member organizations to actively participate in the local work – because that is where we can work across disciplines to benefit all members of the organizations.

2.  **Growth, Welfare, and Employment**
It is crucial for the Danish social model that economic policy supports sound and sustainable public finances, and that the private and public sectors support each other. Growth and welfare are mutually dependent, as are the public and private sectors. This should be the basis for economic policy and the development of public welfare. The trade union should therefore support good framework conditions and meaningful regulation of both the competitive conditions of companies and the public sector, and the public economy must be ensured through fair tax-based financing. Denmark is a very egalitarian society internationally, but unfortunately, inequality has gradually increased in recent years, which can reduce social cohesion. The trade union will therefore work actively for a more egalitarian Denmark. It is important for the trade union to ensure a dynamic labor market with high employment, security, and inclusivity, as well as good opportunities for upskilling and career changes. The trade union should work to ensure that more people on the margins of the labor market become part of the workforce, and to strengthen and develop the flexicurity model in the form of a contract-based labor market with a robust and attractive unemployment insurance system and an active labor market policy that also provides good support for new types of workers. In the active labor market policy, the role of unemployment funds should be strengthened with the industry-specific expertise that unemployment funds possess. A secure labor market is particularly important when Denmark needs to meet its national and international obligations in the coming years and implement an ambitious green transition that is also socially just and balanced. The trade union should also set an ambitious welfare agenda and work for a strong public sector that ensures equal access to welfare services and a stable and strong economic development. The public sector must keep pace with population growth and match the level of prosperity in society. It is important to ensure the perspective of employees in the political debate about economic policy and productivity. In close coordination with member organizations, there should be increased focus on how research, digitalization, and automation, in conjunction with well-trained employees, are the prerequisites for continued good productivity and wage development. It is essential that there are good opportunities to retire from the labor market, which ensure dignity and equality across employee and education groups, and which are compatible with a healthy public economy and continued development of the welfare state. The trade union views public retirement schemes as a whole, consisting of early retirement, senior pension, the right to early retirement, and the folk pension. In addition, there is the after-work pension.

With the introduction of early retirement and senior pensions, retirement options have become more equitable for Danish employees. The union must work to ensure a well-functioning pension system, where the folkepension (national pension) and ATP (a supplementary pension scheme) provide basic retirement income, and where it is worthwhile to save for the collectively agreed pension schemes that help prevent a significant decline in living standards upon retirement. The union is also working to promote good senior arrangements for employees who want to, and have the opportunity to, continue working, either full-time or part-time, after the statutory retirement age.

3. Education and Skills Development
Both the public and private labor markets depend on being able to recruit qualified employees. The union must work to ensure increased public investment in all levels of education, and to develop and strengthen basic, vocational, and higher education, so that employees acquire the skills needed in the future labor market. There is a growing need for adults throughout their working lives and at all levels of education to acquire both professional and general, as well as personal, skills, and for adults to have better opportunities to change careers during their working lives. Skills development is crucial for keeping up with the demands of the labor market, especially for unskilled workers. The union must work to ensure that education and skills development are a real opportunity for everyone, regardless of geography, educational background, gender, age, or employment status, and that economic opportunities and frameworks are provided that are motivating for both employers and employees to invest in skills development in both basic and further education systems. The union must work to ensure that all adults have the basic skills and competencies necessary to succeed in the labor market. Special efforts must be made for unskilled citizens. At the same time, efforts must be made to retrain members whose jobs are made obsolete or otherwise affected by digitalization, so that they can acquire skills in new areas, and especially in the field of green transition. The union must work to ensure that as many young people as possible transition to vocational education after primary school, and that special efforts are made for the group of young people who are not immediately ready to begin vocational education. The union must also work to increase enrollment in vocational schools, vocational academies, and professional bachelor programs, to ensure the necessary qualified workforce for the Danish labor market. There is a particular need to promote a better balance between the supply of education and the demand for qualified workers in the labor market. The size of educational programs must be adjusted to meet future needs. The union must work to further develop the VEU (Vocational Education and Training) system to promote a coherent VEU system that prioritizes activities targeted at unskilled and skilled workers, and that ensures better frameworks and sufficient state co-funding for further education at the tertiary level. The union must work to strengthen the influence and involvement of stakeholders in all parts of the education system, with a particular focus on areas relevant to employees and the needs of the labor market. The union must support and strengthen the employee representatives on the boards and committees of educational institutions.

4. Labor Law and Working Life
The union must contribute to ensuring and developing the Danish labor market model. It is therefore essential to ensure that the negotiating results achieved by member organizations are implemented through the enforcement of collective agreements. This requires the union to assist member organizations in handling their cases in the field of labor law. The union must also continue to assist in the negotiation and renewal of collective agreements. The Danish model is being challenged in several areas by the globalized and digital society. At the same time, social dumping is a threat to the Danish model and challenges the hard-won collective agreement rights and wage development in Denmark. The same applies to new and atypical forms of employment, including platform-based work, where there may be uncertainty about their employment law status. It is crucial for the union to ensure that work is carried out under fair conditions. Collective agreements, public regulation, and government oversight of violations of laws and working conditions must provide the necessary safeguards against social dumping and the exploitation of employees in any form. It is also still necessary to ensure a safe and healthy working environment.

A stable and effective labor inspection agency is needed, but there is also a need to rethink and strengthen the efforts of trade unions and the overall social dialogue system in the area of workplace safety, with a focus on creating visible changes in individual workplaces, so that a good work-life balance becomes part of the conditions necessary to ensure that employees do not suffer physical or psychological strain. The trade union must work to promote equality, with a focus on equal pay, equal sharing of leave, breaking down gender segregation in the labor market and education, and achieving a balance between family and work life. The trade union must work against discrimination and unfair treatment in the workplace.

5. The EU and the International Arena:
In a globalized world, where many challenges require cross-border solutions, strong international cooperation is essential if we are to continue to shape the future of the labor market and ensure that it is controlled by people, for people. The trade union must ensure its presence and influence in the most important international forums and organizations, as well as in international labor cooperation. The trade union will work within the Nordic, European, and international labor movement, and support initiatives that promote social dialogue, good working conditions, and protect workers' rights, taking into account the Danish labor market model.

The EU contributes to creating a framework that ensures fair competition, a decent working environment, and promotes equality, but there are also areas where the EU should not interfere, such as in the areas of wage determination and labor relations, which are best addressed by the social partners at the national level. In assessing EU initiatives, continued respect for the Danish labor market model is crucial. Freedom of movement must take place under conditions that do not lead to social dumping, and competition between Danish, European, and international companies must take place on fair terms. Therefore, the trade union will work to ensure that the EU contributes solutions to cross-border challenges related to social dumping and atypical employment conditions through better regulations and effective enforcement of existing rules. At the same time, the trade union must work to ensure that workers' rights are not subordinated to the economic freedoms of the internal market.
