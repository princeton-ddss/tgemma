AWS's victory in the parliamentary elections created an opportunity to fully implement this program. The beginning of this four-year term presents the union with new and challenging tasks. The fate of Polish workers is increasingly dependent on the development of the international situation and the globalization of the world economy. Poland, already a member of the OECD and negotiating its accession to the European Union, is integrating into its structures and becoming subject to its rules and regulations. The situation in the country is undergoing fundamental changes as a result of the implemented constitutional reforms: changes to the administrative structure and decentralization of the state, reforms of education, social security, healthcare, and the introduction of health insurance. The economic transformation continues, characterized by a reduction in the role of traditional sectors, especially heavy industry, due to economic restructuring, the development of trade and services, and an increase in private ownership. The result of these changes is not only rapid economic growth, technological and organizational progress, but also persistent unemployment, increasing social inequality, deregulation of industrial relations, and a decline in union membership. These conditions require a redefinition of the union's role in economic and social policy, in its relationships with new partners in public life and the labor market.
