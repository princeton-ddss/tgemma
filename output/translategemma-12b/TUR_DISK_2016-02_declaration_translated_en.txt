Especially after the global crisis that began in 2008, it has once again been proven that crises are a structural feature of capitalism. The neoliberal ideology, which has been promoted by capital circles for the past 30 years, claiming that "a free market will cure all ills," has collapsed. Globalization has brought only poverty, unemployment, war, and destruction to the world's people. The network of national and international relations that emerges in the interests of transnational capital possesses a quality not dissimilar to the conditions of "wild capitalism" that were used to describe its early stages. In the pursuit of "maximum profit," global capital has commodified everything, leaving no asset or value untouched. A world has been created where even the labor of women and children is exploited mercilessly, and migrant workers are subjected to inhumane conditions. This process has profoundly impacted labor-capital relations and production relations as a whole. Transnational capital has relied on a policy of developing competitiveness through unorganized and cheap labor, and an attempt has been made to establish a "market economy without rules" on a global scale.
