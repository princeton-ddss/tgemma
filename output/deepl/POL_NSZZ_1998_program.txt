The AWS victory in the parliamentary elections created an opportunity to fully implement this program. The beginning of the four-year term poses new and difficult challenges for the union. The fate of Polish workers is increasingly dependent on international developments and the globalization of the world economy. Poland, already a member of the OECD and negotiating accession to the European Union, is becoming integrated into its structures and increasingly subject to its rules.  The situation in the country is undergoing fundamental changes as a result of the political reforms being introduced: changes in the administrative structure and decentralization of the state, reforms in education, social security, health care, and the introduction of health insurance. The economic transformation continues, characterized by a decline in the role of traditional sectors, especially heavy industry, as a result of economic restructuring, the development of trade and services, and an increase in private ownership.  The result of these changes is not only rapid economic growth and technological and organizational progress, but also persistent unemployment, increasing social stratification, deregulation of industrial relations, and declining union membership.  These conditions require a redefinition of the role of trade unions in relation to economic and social policy, and in relation to new partners in public life and on the labor market.
