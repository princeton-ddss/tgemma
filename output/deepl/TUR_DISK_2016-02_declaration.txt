Especially after the global crisis that began in 2008, it has been proven once again that crises are a structural feature of capitalism. The neoliberal ideology, which has been promoted by capital circles for the past 30 years, claiming that “the free market is the cure for all ills,” has collapsed. The only things globalization has brought to the peoples of the world are poverty, unemployment, war, and destruction. The network of national and international relations that has emerged in line with the interests of transnational capital is no different in nature from the conditions of “wild capitalism” used in the early days of capitalism. In pursuit of its goal of “maximum profit,” global capital has turned everything into a commodity to be bought and sold. It has created a world where even the labor of women and children is ruthlessly exploited and migrant workers face inhumane conditions. This process has profoundly affected labor-capital relations and production relations as a whole. Transnational capital has relied on a policy of developing its competitive power through unorganized and cheap labor, and a “market economy without rules” has been attempted to be made dominant worldwide.
