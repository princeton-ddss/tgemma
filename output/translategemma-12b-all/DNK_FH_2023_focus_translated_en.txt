1.  **Building a Stronger Union: A Shared Commitment**

The union membership rate in the Danish labor market has been declining for over 25 years. Today, only 50% of the workforce is a member of a union or affiliated organization, and in the private sector, it is now more common to be unorganized than organized.

The member organizations of the union are responsible for recruiting and retaining members, and this is an ongoing effort. However, there is a common challenge – across industries, education levels, sectors, and age groups – that requires a coordinated effort. This effort should complement the work of member organizations in reversing the decline in membership.

Therefore, a key goal is to reverse the decline in union membership during the upcoming term. In close collaboration with member organizations, the union will develop and implement initiatives to support the efforts of member organizations to recruit and retain members.

This will involve creating a forum focused on recruitment and retention, where knowledge and experiences can be shared, and progress can be tracked. The goal is also to conduct new analyses and develop new initiatives to ensure that fewer members are lost and to focus on initiatives targeted at young people. The principle is that every new or retained member is in the best interest of the union.

The union will also support efforts to ensure consistency between central initiatives and the work of local unions, and encourage member organizations to actively participate in local efforts. This is where cross-industry collaboration can benefit all members.

**2. Growth, Welfare, and Employment**

A strong Danish social model relies on economic policies that support sound public finances, and where both the private and public sectors support each other. Growth and welfare are interdependent, as are the public and private sectors. This should be the foundation of economic policy and the development of the welfare state.

The union will therefore support good framework conditions and meaningful regulations for both businesses and the public sector, and ensure that the public finances are based on a fair tax system.

Denmark is a relatively equal society, but unfortunately, inequality has been gradually increasing in recent years, which can reduce social cohesion. The union will therefore work actively to create a more equitable society.

It is important for the union to ensure a dynamic labor market with high employment, security, and inclusivity, as well as good opportunities for upskilling and career changes. The union will work to ensure that more people on the margins of the labor market become part of the workforce, and to strengthen and develop the flexicurity model, which is based on a collective bargaining agreement, a robust and attractive unemployment insurance system, and an active labor market policy that provides support for new types of workers. The role of unemployment funds (A-kasser) should be strengthened with industry-specific expertise.

A secure labor market is particularly important as Denmark strives to meet its national and international obligations and implement an ambitious green transition that is also socially just and balanced.

The union will also promote a proactive welfare agenda and work towards a strong public sector that ensures equal access to social services and a stable and robust economic development. The public sector must keep pace with population growth and the overall development of the economy.

It is important to ensure that the perspective of employees is included in the political debate on economic policy and productivity. In close coordination with member organizations, there should be a greater focus on how research, digitalization, and automation, in conjunction with well-trained employees, are essential for continued productivity and wage growth.

It is crucial to have good retirement options that ensure dignity and equality across different employee and education groups, and that are compatible with a healthy public economy and the continued development of the welfare state.

The union views public retirement schemes as a whole, consisting of early retirement, senior pension, the right to early retirement, and old-age pension.

**3. Labor Law and Work-Life Balance**

The union will contribute to ensuring and developing the Danish labor market model. This requires ensuring that the results achieved through negotiations by member organizations are implemented through enforcement of collective bargaining agreements. The union must assist member organizations in pursuing their cases in labor law matters. The union will also continue to assist in the negotiation and renewal of collective bargaining agreements.

The Danish model is challenged in many ways by globalization and the digital society. Social dumping is also a threat to the Danish model and challenges the hard-won collective bargaining rights and wage development in Denmark. This also applies to new and atypical forms of employment, including platform-based work, where there may be uncertainty about their legal employment status.

It is essential for the union to ensure that work is carried out under fair conditions. Collective agreements, public regulations, and government oversight of violations of laws and work clauses must provide the necessary safeguards against social dumping and the exploitation of workers in all forms.

It is also essential to ensure a safe and healthy working environment. There is a need for a stable and effective labor inspection agency, but there is also a need to rethink and strengthen the union's and the overall social partners' own efforts in occupational safety, with a focus on creating visible changes in the workplace, so that a good work-life balance becomes part of the conditions that ensure that workers are not physically and psychologically stressed.

The union will work for equality, focusing on equal pay, equal sharing of leave, opening up the gender-divided labor market and education system, and balancing family and work life. The union will work to combat discrimination and unfair treatment in the workplace.

**4. The EU and the International Arena**

In a globalized world, where many challenges require cross-border solutions, a strong international union collaboration is essential if we are to continue to shape the future of the labor market and ensure that it is controlled by people for the benefit of people.

The union will ensure its presence and influence in the most important international forums and organizations, and in international union cooperation. The union will work within the Nordic, European, and international labor movements, and support initiatives that promote social dialogue, good working conditions, and protect union rights, taking into account the Danish labor market model.

The EU contributes to creating a framework that ensures fair competition, a decent working environment, and promotes equality, but there are also areas where the EU should not interfere, such as wage determination and labor relations that are best addressed by the social partners at the national level. A continued respect for the Danish labor market model is crucial in the assessment of EU initiatives.

Freedom of movement must take place under conditions that do not lead to social dumping, and competition between Danish, European, and international companies must take place on fair terms.

Therefore, the union will work to ensure that the EU contributes to solutions for cross-border challenges such as social dumping and atypical employment conditions through better regulations and effective enforcement of existing rules. The union will also work to ensure that workers' rights are not subordinate to economic freedoms in the internal market.
