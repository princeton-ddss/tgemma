A skilled workforce. High-quality and accessible education are the foundation of a civilized society. Skills. The trade union wants to strengthen everyone's right to learn throughout life and their working career. Good skills and the opportunity for continuous learning throughout one's career strengthen equal opportunities to participate in working life. Investing in skills strengthens employment, prevents social exclusion, and improves equality and fairness in society. Investments in skills and employee well-being create the conditions for improving productivity. The green transition and technological development are rapidly changing the skills needs and professional structures of working life. The importance of skills is highlighted in ensuring a just transition. The actual opportunity for employees to improve their education and skills or to train for a new profession must be improved. There is a shortage of skilled workers in almost all sectors and regions. In addition to highly educated specialists, there is also a shortage of professional workers in various fields. In addition to strengthening the skills of Finns, it is necessary to increase labor and education-based immigration and to fully utilize the skills of international workers. Workplace as a key learning environment. A workplace can thrive and function productively when everyone's skills are recognized and utilized, and when it is possible to develop skills. Workplaces need to implement measures that promote a learning-oriented culture and strengthen collective learning. New and better products and working methods emerge in workplaces through the development of skills. Opportunities for developing skills are distributed very unevenly in workplaces. Employers' investments in the development of employees' skills have been declining for some time. The systematic development and management of skills in workplaces must be strengthened by implementing skills assessments. In addition, the employer must draw up a comprehensive training plan for the entire workplace every year. The opportunities for individuals to improve their own skills and education levels during their working lives must be strengthened. Changing skills needs. The EU's goal is that 60 percent of the working-age population will participate in continuous learning every year. Achieving this goal requires determined action to ensure a attractive and high-quality continuous learning offer and guidance services. Participation in continuous learning should be possible for all working-age people regardless of their financial situation. The barriers to accessing education must be lowered. Education must be easily accessible and affordable. Education leading to a qualification must be free of charge. Study benefits must ensure sufficient income during studies in different life situations. A new form of support is needed to replace the abolished adult education allowance, which ensures income during a period of study leave. Development of the education system. The long-term development and resourcing of the education system create the foundation for skills and learning. Long-term investments must be made in the well-being of children and young people, early childhood education, basic education, and secondary and higher education. Skilled teachers and other staff, sufficient resources, good working conditions, and leadership are important enablers of high-quality education. In the future, education must be able to better meet the individual support and guidance needs of students. Gender-based choices are clearly visible when young people transition from basic education to vocational and higher education. A diverse and high-quality understanding of working life is needed to mitigate gender-based educational choices. Students' and young people's mental health problems have increased. Education must strengthen a sense of community, participatory practices, and learner-centeredness. Sustainable economy. The trade union believes that the economy should support a just, sustainable, and prosperous Finland. Strengthening employment and skills, the sustainability of the tax and pension system, and combating climate change are key to this. Supporting economic growth. Finland's aging population and the economic downturn caused by the coronavirus crisis require Finland to take measures to support economic growth. Economic recovery measures and investments should be directed to support new growth sectors, climate policy goals, and skills development. Adequate funding for research and innovation is needed to utilize new technologies and digitalization for projects that create new jobs. This can improve Finland's competitiveness and its ability to respond to structural changes. Sustainable taxation. The focus of taxation should be shifted from taxes on labor to taxes on property and negative externalities. In particular, the purchasing power of low- and middle-income earners must be protected through taxation. At the same time, the taxation of capital income and wealth must be developed to support the tax base. Taxation should also encourage the promotion of climate action. Sustainable taxation - views on the development of taxation. High employment. The Finnish welfare model is based on a high employment rate. In recent years, the employment rate has increased, but we still need to take measures to increase it in the long term. High employment requires active investment in labor market policy and effective, well-targeted, and high-quality employment services. To speed up the employment of those who are difficult to employ, such as the elderly, young people, immigrants, and people with disabilities, it is necessary to strengthen personalized services. The resources for labor market services in Finland must be brought to the Nordic level. A shortened working week would speed up the return to part-time work for laid-off, dismissed, or otherwise unemployed workers. Consideration should be given to temporarily eliminating employer social security contributions or providing wage subsidies, for example, when hiring immigrants or unemployed people over 55. In a rapidly changing world and economy, we need better security during transitions to speed up the re-employment of those who are laid off. This applies especially to improving the training opportunities for those who are laid off. International recruitment should be seen as a way to increase the employment rate. The processing of work-based residence permit applications should be streamlined, and national recruitment campaigns are needed for sectors with labor shortages. Green transition. Climate change has been a central theme of international cooperation and policy for a long time, and its importance is growing in the future. Climate change. Political decisions made to slow down climate change, together with technological development, have led to a green transition in economies around the world. The transition from fossil fuels to emissions-free energy in energy production, transport, and industry leads to many changes in the economy and working life. At the same time, this is important for the Finnish trade union movement. Moving away from fossil energy. The green transition leads to changes in the economy and working life and creates new opportunities for investment and new jobs. For Finland, the green transition offers an opportunity to replace a large part of fossil fuel imports with new products and services for export. This requires Finland to make bold political decisions and, above all, to invest in skills. Finland is at the forefront of the world in the production of clean and affordable energy. This gives us a competitive advantage on which to build an investment-friendly environment. Breaking away from fossil fuels also supports Finland's self-sufficiency and reduces the impact of geopolitical crises. A just transition. For workers, an important aspect of the green transition is its fairness. A just transition ensures that: workers have the opportunity to upgrade their skills to new professions if necessary, the costs of the transition are not excessively high for any group of people, the benefits are distributed as fairly as possible. Finland has the opportunity to succeed in the green transition, but international competition is fierce and the pace of change is rapid. In addition to affordable and clean energy, it is crucial how we succeed in increasing green technology products and services based on skills. By investing in skills, we also keep workers as involved as possible in the changes and provide them with financial and social support, and support the realization of a just transition in terms of economics, society, and region. This ensures the broad political support needed for the green transition among citizens.
